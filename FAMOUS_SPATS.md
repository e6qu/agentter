## Academic Disputes & Priority Claims

### Gary Marcus vs. Yann LeCun: The CNN Credit Controversy

**Participants:**
- **Gary Marcus** — Cognitive scientist, NYU professor emeritus, founder of Robust.AI
- **Yann LeCun** — Chief AI Scientist at Meta, Turing Award recipient (2018)

**The Dispute:**

In April 2023, Gary Marcus published a scathing Substack article titled **"The False Glorification of Yann LeCun"**, accusing LeCun of systematic "plagiarism of ideas" — defined by the US Office of Research Integrity as "appropriating someone else's idea... without giving credit to its originator."

**Key Claims:**

Marcus argued that LeCun has claimed credit for five major ideas he did not originate:

1. **Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)** — Marcus cited that the foundational work was done by Kunihiko Fukushima (1979-1980), and Wei Zhang et al. (1988) beat LeCun to adding backpropagation to CNNs by nearly a year
2. **Critique of Large Language Models** — Marcus claimed he was first to challenge GPT-2 and GPT-3 in 2019, while LeCun was "publicly hostile to those critiques"
3. **Critique of Scaling Hypothesis** — Marcus stated he doubted scaling publicly in 2022, while LeCun "trolled my critique on Facebook"
4. **World Models** — Marcus traced the concept to Herb Simon's General Problem Solver (1950s) and Schmidhuber's work (1990)
5. **Common Sense and Physical Reasoning** — Marcus cited Ernest Davis and John McCarthy's earlier work

**Direct Quotes:**

> "LeCun has, without a doubt made genuine contributions to AI... But he has also systematically dismissed and ignored the work of others for years, including Schmidhuber, Fukushima, Zhang, Bender, Li and myself, in order to exaggerate his own contributions."
> — Gary Marcus, Substack, April 2023

> "LeCun was, at the time, publicly hostile to those critiques, accusing me of a 'rearguard action.'"
> — Gary Marcus

LeCun's response, when engaging at all, has generally been dismissive. In prior exchanges, LeCun stated:

> "I don't engage in vacuous debates. I build stuff. You should try it sometimes."
> — Yann LeCun (via Wired, May 2023)

**Sources:**
- Gary Marcus, "The False Glorification of Yann LeCun", Substack, April 2023
- Wired, "Gary Marcus Used to Call AI Stupid—Now He Calls It Dangerous", May 5, 2023
- https://garymarcus.substack.com/p/the-false-glorification-of-yann-lecun

### Jürgen Schmidhuber vs. The Turing Trio

**Participants:**
- **Jürgen Schmidhuber** — LSTM co-inventor, "Father of Modern AI" (per some sources)
- **Geoffrey Hinton, Yann LeCun, Yoshua Bengio** — Turing Award recipients (2018)

**The Dispute:**

Since the 2018 Turing Award was announced, Schmidhuber has maintained an ongoing public campaign arguing that the "Deep Learning Trio" (HLB) systematically failed to cite prior work—including his own and others'. He published an 88-page report documenting 17 specific priority disputes.

**Major Claims:**

| Technology | Schmidhuber's Claim | HLB Attribution |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|
| LSTM (1997) | Co-invented with Hochreiter | Often cited but credit diffuse |
| GANs (2014) | His 1990 "Artificial Curiosity" principle | Ian Goodfellow (Bengio's student) |
| ResNets (2015) | His "Highway Networks" (2015) | Kaiming He et al. |
| Transformers (2017) | His 1991 "Fast Weight Programmers" | Vaswani et al. |
| Self-supervised Learning | His 1990-91 work | LeCun claims credit |
| Meta-learning | His 1987 diploma thesis | Bengio et al. (1991) |
| Vanishing gradient analysis | Hochreiter's 1991 thesis | Bengio et al. (1994) |

**Direct Quotes:**

> "They frequently re-published others' work without proper attribution. Even worse, they failed to correct this in subsequent publications. In science, this is unacceptable... They should be stripped of their awards."
> — Jürgen Schmidhuber, interview with Jazzyear, 2024

> "I think Jeff is a bit of a tool and Satya is not..."
> — Elon Musk in email to Sam Altman about Schmidhuber (unrelated but illustrative of the tone of disputes)

**The NIPS 2016 Incident:**

At NIPS 2016, Schmidhuber publicly confronted Ian Goodfellow during his GAN tutorial:

> "[Schmidhuber] stood up and spoke for about two minutes, explaining that he had introduced similar ideas in 1992 under the name Predictability Minimization... Goodfellow responded: 'We've discussed this before, and I published a response to you. I don't think this is the appropriate venue to continue that discussion.'"
> — Quora recounting, 2017

### Schmidhuber vs. Ian Goodfellow: The GAN Dispute

**Participants:**
- **Jürgen Schmidhuber** — IDSIA, LSTM co-inventor
- **Ian Goodfellow** — GAN inventor, then Bengio's student at Université de Montréal

**The Dispute:**

When Goodfellow submitted the first GAN paper to NIPS 2014, Schmidhuber was one of three reviewers—and the only one to reject it. His critique: GANs were essentially his 1992 "Predictability Minimization" (PM) models run in reverse.

Schmidhuber's review noted:

> "The two methods both use 'adversarial' MLPs to estimate certain probabilities and learn encodings of distributions. The difference is that the new system learns to generate non-trivial distributions from statistically independent random inputs, while the old PM learns to generate statistically independent random outputs in response to non-trivial distributions... Is this the main difference? Should GANs be called 'inverse PM'?"

Goodfellow ultimately added a comparison to PM in the final version, but the dispute continued for years, culminating in the NIPS 2016 confrontation.

**Sources:**
- Quora discussions of NIPS 2016 incident
- Goodfellow et al., "Generative Adversarial Networks", NIPS 2014

### The Scaling Laws Debate: Kaplan vs. Chinchilla

**Participants:**
- **Jared Kaplan et al.** — OpenAI researchers, authors of "Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models" (2020)
- **Jordan Hoffmann et al.** — DeepMind researchers, authors of the "Chinchilla" paper (2022)
- **The broader ML community** — Labs that built models based on one set of laws or the other

**The Dispute:**

In January 2020, Kaplan et al. at OpenAI published influential scaling laws suggesting that model performance improves predictably with scale, and that model size should be scaled approximately 3x faster than dataset size. This became the blueprint for GPT-3 (175B parameters) and subsequent large models: make the model as big as possible, and don't worry too much about training data volume.

In March 2022, DeepMind's Hoffmann et al. published "Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models," which directly challenged Kaplan's conclusions. Their key finding: for a given compute budget, model size and training data should be scaled in a 1:1 ratio, not the 3:1 ratio Kaplan had suggested. This meant that nearly every major LLM built between 2020 and 2022 had been trained incorrectly -- too many parameters, too little data.

**The Core Disagreement:**

| Aspect | Kaplan et al. (2020) | Hoffmann et al. / Chinchilla (2022) |
|--------|---------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Optimal scaling ratio | N (parameters) scaled ~3x faster than D (tokens) | N and D should scale in 1:1 ratio |
| Formula | N_optimal proportional to C^0.73 | N_optimal proportional to C^0.50 |
| Implication | Build the biggest model you can | Train a smaller model on much more data |
| Models that followed | GPT-3 (175B), Gopher (280B), Megatron (530B) | Chinchilla (70B), LLaMA (7B-65B) |

**The Proof:**

DeepMind demonstrated their thesis by training Chinchilla -- a 70B parameter model trained on 1.4 trillion tokens, using the same compute budget as their 280B parameter Gopher model. Chinchilla outperformed Gopher on nearly every benchmark, despite being 4x smaller. The implication was devastating: Gopher, GPT-3, and Megatron-Turing NLG had all been massively undertrained relative to their parameter counts.

**Why Kaplan Was Wrong:**

A 2024 replication study identified the root cause of the discrepancy: Kaplan et al. counted non-embedding rather than total parameters, and their analysis relied on "early-stopped" loss values from longer training runs rather than final converged values. These methodological choices systematically biased their scaling coefficients toward overvaluing model size and undervaluing training data.

**Impact on the Industry:**

The Chinchilla paper triggered a paradigm shift. Meta's LLaMA family (2023) was explicitly designed following compute-optimal principles, with LLaMA-7B achieving competitive performance on many benchmarks despite being 25x smaller than GPT-3. The paper essentially proved that the industry had wasted billions of dollars in compute by building models that were too large and feeding them too little data.

Ironically, post-Chinchilla models like LLaMA-2 went even further than Chinchilla recommended, training on 1-2 trillion tokens -- far more than the "optimal" amount -- because inference cost considerations (a factor neither Kaplan nor Hoffmann had modeled) favor smaller, more heavily trained models.

**Resolution:**

The debate is largely settled in Chinchilla's favor for the core question, though a 2024 paper ("Reconciling Kaplan and Chinchilla Scaling Laws") showed that simulating Kaplan's methodology under controlled conditions reproduces Kaplan's biased coefficients, confirming Chinchilla's estimates while explaining why Kaplan got different numbers. The field has moved on to "inference-optimal" scaling, which accounts for deployment costs and favors even smaller, more overtrained models.

**Sources:**
- Kaplan et al., "Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models", arXiv:2001.08361, January 2020
- Hoffmann et al., "Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models", arXiv:2203.15556, March 2022
- Porian et al., "Reconciling Kaplan and Chinchilla Scaling Laws", arXiv:2406.12907, June 2024
- Epoch AI, "Chinchilla Scaling: A Replication Attempt", 2024
- Lifearchitect.ai, "Chinchilla data-optimal scaling laws: In plain English"

## AI Safety & Existential Risk Debates

### Yann LeCun vs. Eliezer Yudkowsky: The Doomer Debate

**Participants:**
- **Yann LeCun** — Meta Chief AI Scientist
- **Eliezer Yudkowsky** — AI safety researcher, founder of MIRI, "doomer" advocate

**The Dispute:**

In April 2023, LeCun and Yudkowsky engaged in a lengthy Twitter debate about AI existential risk. LeCun argued that AI safety concerns were overblown; Yudkowsky argued that LeCun was dangerously dismissive of extinction risks.

**Direct Quotes:**

> "To *guarantee* that a system satisfies objectives, you make it optimize those objectives at run time (what I propose). That solves the problem of aligning behavior to objectives. Then you need to align objectives with human values. But that's not as hard as you make it to be."
> — Yann LeCun, April 2023

> "So you're staking the life of everyone on Earth that: Future AIs are as human-friendly on average as the humans making up corporations; AIs don't collude among themselves better than human corporations; AIs never go beyond superhuman to supercorporate."
> — Eliezer Yudkowsky

> "Stop it, Eliezer. Your scaremongering is already hurting some people. You'll be sorry if it starts getting people killed."
> — Yann LeCun

> "A high-school student actually wrote to me saying that he got into a deep depression after reading prophecies of AI-fueled apocalypse."
> — Yann LeCun

> "As much as 'tu quoque' would be a valid reply... I can't help but observe that you're a lead employee at... Facebook. You've contributed to a *lot* of cases of teenage depression, if that's considered an issue of knockdown importance."
> — Eliezer Yudkowsky

> "The hard take-off is already happening. Just zoom out a little."
> — Elon Musk, joining the debate

> "The 'hard take-off' scenario is utterly impossible."
> — Yann LeCun

**The Meta-Commentary:**

The debate was extensively analyzed by Zvi Mowshowitz, who wrote:

> "Yann seems to literally be saying 'we can create superintelligent beings and it will pose zero risk to humanity because we have corporate law. Saying otherwise is dangerous and wrong and needs to stop.' This is Obvious Nonsense."

**Sources:**
- The Zvi Substack, "Transcript and Brief Response to Twitter Conversation between Yann LeCun and Eliezer Yudkowsky", April 26, 2023
- https://thezvi.substack.com/p/transcript-and-brief-response-to

### Elon Musk vs. Geoffrey Hinton: Royal Society Expulsion

**Participants:**
- **Elon Musk** — CEO of Tesla, SpaceX, xAI; former OpenAI co-founder
- **Geoffrey Hinton** — Nobel Prize winner (Physics, 2024), Turing Award recipient

**The Dispute:**

In March 2025, Hinton called for Musk's expulsion from the Royal Society, citing "the huge damage he is doing to scientific institutions in the US." This followed Musk's attacks on Dr. Anthony Fauci and his labeling of UK MP Jess Phillips as a "rape genocide apologist."

**Direct Quotes:**

> "I think Elon Musk should be expelled from the British Royal Society. Not because he peddles conspiracy theories and makes Nazi salutes, but because of the huge damage he is doing to scientific institutions in the US. Now let's see if he really believes in free speech."
> — Geoffrey Hinton, X post, March 2, 2025

> "Only craven, insecure fools care about awards and memberships. History is the actual judge, always and forever. Your comments above are carelessly ignorant, cruel and false."
> — Elon Musk, response on X, March 2, 2025

**Background:**

The dispute is particularly notable because Hinton and Musk were previously aligned on AI safety concerns. However, their relationship deteriorated—Hinton told the Globe and Mail he made up an excuse to end a call with Musk after 20 minutes when Musk "started rambling" while recruiting him for an AI safety advisory board.

Hinton has also criticized Musk's business practices:

> "I think the big companies are betting on it causing massive job replacement by AI, because that's where the big money is going to be... Elon will get richer and a lot of people get unemployed and Elon won't care."
> — Geoffrey Hinton, Bloomberg TV interview, November 2025

**Sources:**

### Andrew Ng vs. AI Safety Advocates: The "Doomer" Backlash

**Participants:**
- **Andrew Ng** — Google Brain co-founder, Stanford professor, Coursera co-founder, AI Fund founder
- **Geoffrey Hinton** — Nobel Prize winner, former Google researcher
- **Yoshua Bengio** — Turing Award recipient, Mila founder
- **Various AI safety advocates** — Center for AI Safety signatories, policy researchers

**The Dispute:**

Beginning in mid-2023, Andrew Ng emerged as one of the most prominent voices arguing that AI existential risk concerns were not only overblown but actively harmful. His position placed him in direct opposition to former colleagues Hinton and Bengio, who had signed the Center for AI Safety's statement warning that AI could cause human extinction. Ng argued that these fears were being weaponized by large AI companies to push for regulations that would crush open-source competitors and consolidate power among incumbents.

**Direct Quotes:**

> "In the case of extinction risk, I just don't get it -- I don't see any plausible path for AI to lead to human extinction."
> -- Andrew Ng, Financial Times interview, October 2023

> "My greatest fear for the future of AI is if overhyped risks (such as human extinction) lets tech lobbyists get enacted stifling regulations that suppress open-source and crush innovation."
> -- Andrew Ng, The Batch newsletter, 2023

> "There are definitely large tech companies that would rather not have to compete with open source, so they're creating fear of AI leading to human extinction. That is a weapon for lobbyists to argue for legislation that would be very damaging to the open-source community."
> -- Andrew Ng, Australian Financial Review, October 2023

Ng specifically called out what he saw as a convergence of bad ideas:

> "The bad idea that AI could make us go extinct is merging with the bad idea that a good way to make AI safer is to impose burdensome licensing requirements."
> -- Andrew Ng, Australian Financial Review, October 2023

**Hinton's Response:**

Geoffrey Hinton pushed back directly on X:

> "Andrew Ng is claiming that the idea that AI could make us extinct is a big-tech conspiracy."
> -- Geoffrey Hinton, X post, October 2023

Hinton added that he "left Google so that I could speak freely about the existential threat," implying his concerns were genuine, not corporate strategy.

**Ng's Clarification:**

> "I didn't say it is a conspiracy. But I think overhyped fears about AI leading to human extinction are causing real harm: I'm seeing young students discouraged from entering AI because they don't want to contribute to human extinction. Hype about harm is also being used to promote bad regulation worldwide."
> -- Andrew Ng, X post responding to Hinton, October 2023

**The Broader Battle Lines:**

The debate split the AI pioneers into two camps:

| Position | Key Advocates | Core Argument |
|----------|--------------|---------------|
| **X-Risk is real** | Hinton, Bengio, Stuart Russell | AI systems could become uncontrollable; precautionary regulation needed now |
| **X-Risk is overblown** | Ng, LeCun | Extinction fears are vague and speculative; real harm comes from stifling open-source innovation through regulation |

**Resolution:**

No reconciliation. The two camps remain deeply divided. Ng continues to advocate for open-source AI and against what he sees as regulatory capture, while Hinton and Bengio continue to warn of existential risks. The debate has become a proxy for the broader open-source vs. closed-source struggle in AI.

### Anthropic's "Responsible Scaling" vs. Pause AI

**Participants:**
- **Dario Amodei** — Anthropic CEO
- **Jared Kaplan** — Anthropic Chief Science Officer
- **Holly Elmore** — US director of the Pause AI movement
- **Various AI safety advocates** — Effective altruism community, AI governance researchers

**The Dispute:**

In 2023, Anthropic introduced its Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP), which established capability thresholds that trigger specific safety requirements before a model can be deployed or further scaled. The central promise: Anthropic would never train an AI system unless it could guarantee in advance that its safety measures were adequate. This positioned Anthropic as a middle path between "move fast and break things" and the growing Pause AI movement, which demanded a complete halt to frontier AI development.

The Pause AI movement, led by advocates like Holly Elmore, argued that no amount of "responsible" scaling was sufficient when the technology itself posed existential risks.

**Direct Quotes:**

From Dario Amodei's prepared remarks at the UK AI Safety Summit (2023):

> "Responsible scaling policies ensure that each new model is rigorously evaluated before release, minimizing the chance of unintended consequences."
> -- Dario Amodei, UK AI Safety Summit, November 2023

From Holly Elmore (Pause AI):

> "Developing more and more powerful AI without safety regulations in place is not safe. Period."
> -- Holly Elmore, Pause AI protest statement, 2024

**The 2026 Reversal:**

In February 2026, Anthropic dramatically revised its RSP (Version 3.0), dropping the central pledge to pause development if safety could not be guaranteed. The new policy stated:

> "If one AI developer paused development to implement safety measures while others moved forward training and deploying AI systems without strong mitigations, that could result in a world that is less safe."
> -- Anthropic, Responsible Scaling Policy v3.0, February 2026

Anthropic's Chief Science Officer Jared Kaplan explained:

> "We felt that it wouldn't actually help anyone for us to stop training AI models."
> -- Jared Kaplan, interview with TIME, February 2026

The new policy committed instead to: transparency about safety risks, matching or surpassing competitors' safety efforts, and "delaying" development only if Anthropic believed itself to be the leader in the AI race and the risks of catastrophe to be significant.

**Reactions:**

The revision was widely criticized by AI safety advocates as abandoning the company's founding promise. Pause AI activists who had already been protesting outside Anthropic's offices viewed the change as proof that market incentives would always override safety commitments.

Critics noted the timing: the policy change came during a period of intense competition with OpenAI, including the Pentagon contracting dispute (see Sam Altman vs. Dario Amodei section).

**The Fundamental Tension:**

| Position | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Responsible Scaling** | Safety research must happen at the frontier, inside labs building the most capable models; unilateral pauses cede ground to less safety-conscious developers |
| **Pause AI** | Iterative correction is insufficient for potentially catastrophic technology; the race dynamic ensures no lab will voluntarily slow down; only binding regulation can work |
| **Critics of both** | "Responsible scaling" is PR cover for racing ahead; "pause" is impractical and ignores geopolitical competition |

**Resolution:**

Unresolved and intensifying. Anthropic's RSP revision validated the Pause AI movement's prediction that voluntary commitments would erode under competitive pressure, while simultaneously illustrating Anthropic's argument that unilateral action was futile without industry-wide regulation.

### The Anthropic Split: Dario Amodei and the OpenAI Exodus (2021)

**Participants:**
- **Dario Amodei** — Former OpenAI VP of Research, Anthropic co-founder and CEO
- **Daniela Amodei** — Former OpenAI VP of Safety and Policy, Anthropic co-founder and President
- **Tom Brown** — Former OpenAI researcher, led GPT-3 engineering, Anthropic co-founder
- **Sam Altman** — OpenAI CEO
- **Ilya Sutskever** — OpenAI Chief Scientist (remained at OpenAI until 2024)
- Five additional OpenAI researchers who departed with the Amodeis

**The Dispute:**

In early 2021, Dario Amodei departed OpenAI along with his sister Daniela and approximately seven other senior researchers to found Anthropic. The departure was the largest single talent loss in OpenAI's history and represented a fundamental disagreement about how AI development should proceed in the wake of GPT-3's success.

**The Core Disagreement:**

The Amodei faction believed that the gap between what AI could do and what humans understood about controlling it was widening with each generation of models. Dario's proposal inside OpenAI was to dramatically increase investment in interpretability and alignment research. He felt that OpenAI, particularly after its restructuring from a pure nonprofit to a "capped-profit" entity and its $1 billion Microsoft deal, was prioritizing speed and scale over safety research.

Crucially, Amodei has said the split was not about commercialization per se -- he was directly involved in the commercialization of GPT-3. The disagreement was about the ratio of capabilities research to safety research and the organizational culture around risk.

**Direct Quotes:**

> "It is incredibly unproductive to try and argue with someone else's vision. Take some people you trust and go make your vision happen."
> -- Dario Amodei, explaining why he left rather than fighting internally (Inc., 2024)

> "The real question was: are we doing enough on safety relative to what we're doing on capabilities? And the answer I kept arriving at was no."
> -- Dario Amodei, paraphrased from multiple interviews

Amodei has been careful not to directly attack OpenAI's leadership, framing the departure as a difference in "vision" rather than a personal conflict:

> "I've tried to be diplomatic about it... It wasn't about any one decision. It was about the overall direction and whether I could be most effective from inside or outside."
> -- Dario Amodei, various interviews, 2023-2024

**Who Left:**

The departing group included key contributors to GPT-2 and GPT-3, representing a significant brain drain. Tom Brown, who had led GPT-3's engineering, was among the most notable departures. The group took with them deep expertise in large language model training and a conviction that safety-first development required a new institutional structure.

**Ilya Sutskever's Role:**

Notably, Ilya Sutskever -- who shared many of the safety concerns that motivated the Anthropic founders -- chose to remain at OpenAI in 2021. He would later lead the coup against Altman in November 2023, partially vindicating the concerns that had driven the Amodei group away two years earlier. In his 2024 deposition, Sutskever's secret memo explored the possibility of merging OpenAI with Anthropic after Altman's firing, which would have potentially reunited the two factions under Dario Amodei's leadership.

**Resolution:**

No reconciliation. The split has only deepened into one of the defining rivalries in AI. Anthropic raised over $10 billion in funding and became OpenAI's primary competitor, while the philosophical disagreement about safety vs. speed has become the central fault line in the industry.

**Participants:**

What began as an internal disagreement at OpenAI has evolved into the defining rivalry of the AI era. **The Philosophical Divide:**

| Dimension | Sam Altman (OpenAI) | Dario Amodei (Anthropic) |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Development philosophy** | Move fast, iterate, deploy widely | Move carefully, test rigorously, deploy cautiously |
| **Safety approach** | Safety through deployment feedback and tight iteration loops | Safety through pre-deployment research, interpretability, and constitutional AI |

| **Theory of change** | Empower millions of builders; market forces find equilibrium | Structured research and governance prevent irreversible catastrophe |

## Philosophical & Methodological Disputes

### François Chollet vs. Scaling Maximalists: "LLMs Won't Lead to AGI"

**Participants:**
- **François Chollet** — Keras creator, Google AI researcher, creator of ARC-AGI benchmark
- **Various scaling advocates** — Researchers at OpenAI, Anthropic, and the broader "scaling is all you need" camp

**The Dispute:**

Since 2023, Chollet has been the most prominent critic of the "scaling hypothesis" — the idea that simply making LLMs bigger will produce AGI. He created the ARC-AGI benchmark specifically to test novel reasoning that memorization can't solve, and argued that the entire 2020-2024 pre-training era confused "performance on known tasks with genuine intelligence."

**Key Arguments:**

| Position | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Chollet** | LLMs are sophisticated memorizers, not reasoners. Scaling pre-training is a dead end. AGI requires "test-time adaptation" — models modifying themselves on-the-fly. |
| **Scaling advocates** | Emergent abilities appear at scale. Test-time compute (o1, o3) is just another form of scaling. ARC-AGI is being solved — o3 scored 75-87% in Dec 2024. |
| **Chollet's rebuttal** | o3's ARC score required $10K+ of compute per task. Real intelligence is efficient adaptation, not brute-force search. |

**Direct Quotes:**

> "The pre-training scaling era led the community to confuse performance on known tasks with genuine intelligence. The industry became fixated on the idea that simply cramming more data into bigger models would spontaneously generate AGI."

### Richard Sutton's "Bitter Lesson" and Its Discontents

**Participants:**
- **Richard Sutton** — Founder of reinforcement learning, University of Alberta
- **Rodney Brooks** — Roboticist, iRobot co-founder, MIT professor emeritus
- **Various AI researchers** — Shimon Whiteson (Oxford), Katherine Bailey (Shopify), and others

**The Dispute:**

In March 2019, Sutton published "The Bitter Lesson" — a short essay arguing that the history of AI shows one consistent pattern: general methods that leverage computation always win over clever approaches that incorporate human domain knowledge. The essay became one of the most cited and debated pieces in modern AI discourse.

**The Core Claim:**

> "The biggest lesson that can be read from 70 years of AI research is that general methods that leverage computation are ultimately the most effective, and by a large margin."
> — Richard Sutton, "The Bitter Lesson", 2019

**Notable Rebuttals:**

- **Rodney Brooks** wrote "A Better Lesson," arguing Sutton cherry-picked examples and that embodied intelligence requires domain structure
- **Shimon Whiteson** (Oxford) argued the lesson applies only to narrow tasks, not general intelligence
- **Katherine Bailey** noted that compute scaling has diminishing returns without architectural innovation

**Why It Matters for AI Agents:**

The Bitter Lesson is the philosophical foundation for the scaling hypothesis. The debate directly informs whether AI coding agents should be built with elaborate domain-specific scaffolding (handcrafted prompts, specialized tools) or whether throwing more compute at general models will always win. The current answer appears to be "both" — the most effective agents (Claude Code, Codex CLI) combine general-purpose LLMs with carefully engineered harnesses.

## Regulatory & Policy Battles

### The EU AI Act Controversies: Tech Leaders vs. Regulators

**Participants:**
- **Yann LeCun** — Meta Chief AI Scientist (at the time)
- **Cédric O** — Former French digital minister, open letter co-initiator
- **René Obermann** — Chair of Airbus, open letter co-initiator
- **160+ European executives** — From Renault, Heineken, Airbus, Siemens, and others
- **EU legislators** — European Parliament, European Commission
- **Elon Musk, Sam Altman** — Signatories of the opposing "AI Pause" letter
- **France, Germany, Italy** — National governments that pushed back on foundation model regulation

**The Dispute:**

The EU AI Act, first proposed in 2021 and finalized in late 2023, became the world's first comprehensive AI regulation. Its passage triggered a fierce lobbying battle between those who wanted strict regulation of foundation models (including general-purpose AI systems like ChatGPT) and those who argued such regulation would cripple European innovation and hand competitive advantage to the US and China.

**The Open Letter (June 2023):**

Over 150 European executives, researchers, and investors signed an open letter warning that the Act's approach to foundation models would devastate European competitiveness:

> "In our assessment, the draft legislation would jeopardise Europe's competitiveness and technological sovereignty without effectively tackling the challenges we are and will be facing."
> -- Open letter signed by 160+ European executives, June 2023

The signatories argued that the Act would impose "disproportionate compliance costs and disproportionate liability risks" on companies developing foundation models, regardless of how those models were actually used.

**Yann LeCun's Role:**

LeCun was among the most vocal signatories, framing the debate as a battle for open-source survival:

> "Regulate products, don't regulate technology. Promote open source foundational models. At the very least, don't regulate them in ways that favor incumbents."
> -- Yann LeCun, X post sharing Mistral's position on the EU AI Act, November 2023

After the Act's final negotiations concluded, LeCun praised the outcome on the open-source front:

> "The EU AI Act negotiations ended. One contentious issue was the regulation of foundation models, particularly open source ones. Kudos to the French, German, and Italian governments for not giving up on open source models."
> -- Yann LeCun, X post, December 2023

**The Counter-Position:**

The industry letter ran directly counter to another letter -- the "AI Pause" letter organized by the Future of Life Institute in March 2023, signed by Elon Musk, Yoshua Bengio, and thousands of AI researchers calling for a six-month moratorium on training AI systems more powerful than GPT-4. Sam Altman, while not signing the pause letter, testified before the US Senate in May 2023 calling for government regulation of AI, including licensing requirements for frontier models.

This created a stark divide:

| Faction | Key Figures | Position on EU AI Act |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Anti-regulation** | LeCun, Zuckerberg, Mistral, 150+ European execs | Foundation model rules would kill European AI; regulate applications, not technology |
| **Pro-regulation** | Altman (cautiously), Bengio, EU Commission | Powerful AI requires oversight at the model level, not just the application level |
| **Pause advocates** | Musk (initially), Future of Life Institute | Go further than the EU AI Act; halt frontier development entirely |
| **National governments** | France, Germany, Italy | Protect open-source; avoid rules that entrench US tech giants |

**The Outcome:**

The final EU AI Act (agreed December 2023, entered into force 2024) included tiered regulation based on risk levels, with specific obligations for "general-purpose AI" (GPAI) systems. Open-source models received partial exemptions, a victory for the LeCun-led faction. However, the most powerful models (those trained with more than 10^25 FLOPS) face additional requirements regardless of openness.

**Ongoing Tensions:**

In September 2024, Meta's Yann LeCun co-signed yet another open letter, this time criticizing the EU for blocking Meta from using European user data to train AI models -- arguing this was stifling innovation through privacy regulation rather than AI-specific rules.

### Oracle vs. Google: The Decade-Long Java API Copyright War

**Participants:**
- **Oracle America, Inc.** — Acquired Java through Sun Microsystems purchase (2010)
- **Google LLC** — Used Java API declarations in Android
- **Justice Stephen Breyer** — Wrote the Supreme Court majority opinion
- **Justice Clarence Thomas** — Wrote the dissent
- **The entire software industry** — Watched nervously for a decade

**The Dispute:**

Oracle v. Google (2010-2021) was the most consequential software copyright case in history. The core question: can the structure and declarations of an API — the way programmers call functions — be copyrighted? The answer would determine whether anyone who reimplemented an interface could be sued.

**Background:**

When Google built Android, it reimplemented 37 Java API packages (approximately 11,500 lines of declaring code out of Java's 2.86 million lines) so that the millions of Java programmers worldwide could write Android apps using familiar syntax. Sun Microsystems, Java's creator, had encouraged this kind of adoption. But after Oracle acquired Sun in 2010, Oracle sued Google for $8.8 billion in damages, arguing that copying the API declarations was copyright infringement.

**The Decade-Long Journey:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2010 | Oracle sues Google |
| 2012 | District court rules APIs not copyrightable (Judge Alsup, who learned Java for the case) |
| 2014 | Federal Circuit reverses: APIs are copyrightable |
| 2016 | Jury finds Google's use is fair use |
| 2018 | Federal Circuit reverses the jury again |
| 2021 | Supreme Court rules 6-2 for Google |

**Direct Quotes:**

From Justice Breyer's majority opinion:

> "Where Google reimplemented a user interface, taking only what was needed to allow users to put their accrued talents to work in a new and transformative program, Google's copying of the Sun Java API was a fair use of that material as a matter of law."
> — Justice Stephen Breyer, Google LLC v. Oracle America, Inc., 593 U.S. 1 (2021)

> "To the extent that Google used parts of the Sun Java API to create a new platform that could be readily used by programmers, its use was consistent with that creative 'progress' that is the basic constitutional objective of copyright itself."
> — Justice Breyer

The Court notably ducked the copyrightability question entirely:

> "We shall assume, but purely for argument's sake, that the entire Sun Java API falls within the definition of that which can be copyrighted. We shall ask instead whether Google's use of part of that API was a 'fair use.'"
> — Justice Breyer

From Justice Thomas's fierce dissent:

> "In sum, three of the four statutory fair-use factors weigh decidedly against Google. The nature of the copyrighted work — the sole factor possibly favoring Google — cannot by itself support a determination of fair use because holding otherwise would improperly override Congress' determination that declaring code is copyrightable."
> — Justice Clarence Thomas, dissenting

Oracle was incandescent:

> "The Google platform just got bigger and market power greater. The barriers to entry higher and the ability to compete lower. They stole Java and spent a decade litigating as only a monopolist can."
> — Oracle spokesperson, following the ruling

The EFF celebrated:

> "Today's decision gives more breathing room to software developers who connect with existing platforms and audiences through reimplementing APIs."
> — Electronic Frontier Foundation, April 5, 2021

**Why It Mattered:**

Had Oracle won, every API in computing could theoretically be locked behind copyright. Reimplementing an interface — which is how Wine runs Windows software on Linux, how Postgres provides Oracle compatibility modes, how any competing cloud service provides API-compatible alternatives — would have become legally perilous. The case hung over the entire software industry for eleven years.

**Sources:**
- Supreme Court Opinion, Google LLC v. Oracle America, Inc., 593 U.S. 1 (2021)
- Wikipedia, "Google LLC v. Oracle America, Inc."
- EFF, "Victory for Fair Use: The Supreme Court Reverses the Federal Circuit in Oracle v. Google", April 2021
- Harvard Law Review, Vol. 135, "Google LLC v. Oracle America, Inc."
- CNBC, "Supreme Court rules in Google's favor in copyright dispute with Oracle over Android software", April 5, 2021

### The JSON vs. XML Holy War (2006-2012)

**Participants:**
- **Douglas Crockford** — JSON creator, author of "JavaScript: The Good Parts"
- **XML advocates** — Various enterprise developers and standards bodies

**The Dispute:**

When Crockford proposed JSON as a simpler alternative to XML for data interchange, the XML community was hostile. JSON was dismissed as "a toy format" by enterprise developers who had invested heavily in SOAP, XSLT, and XML Schema.

**The Resolution:**

JSON won completely. By 2015, virtually every new web API used JSON. XML persists in legacy systems, configuration files, and document formats (DOCX, SVG), but the debate is over. This is the rare holy war with a clear winner.

### The systemd Controversy: Lennart Poettering vs. the Unix Traditionalists

**Participants:**
- **Lennart Poettering** — Red Hat developer, creator of systemd and PulseAudio
- **Unix traditionalists** — Sysadmins, Debian developers, and the Devuan project founders
- **Linus Torvalds** — Indirectly implicated by Poettering as the root cause of community toxicity

**The Dispute:**

The adoption of systemd as the default init system across major Linux distributions (Fedora in 2011, Arch in 2012, Debian in 2014, Ubuntu in 2015) triggered one of the most bitter and protracted controversies in Linux history. systemd replaced the traditional SysV init system and its simple shell-script-based startup with a sprawling suite of approximately 70 tightly integrated binaries handling not just initialization but also logging, networking, device management, login sessions, cron jobs, DNS resolution, and more.

**The Technical Argument:**

| Position | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Anti-systemd** | Violates the Unix philosophy of "do one thing and do it well." Creates a monolithic dependency that is impossible to replace piecemeal. Introduces unnecessary complexity and binary logging. |
| **Pro-systemd** | SysV init was broken — race conditions during boot were common, service dependencies failed silently, and startup order was unpredictable. systemd makes Linux faster, more reliable, and more consistent across distributions. |
| **Pragmatists** | systemd won because it solved real problems. The Unix philosophy is a guideline, not a law. |

Critics argued that systemd "flies in the face of the Unix concept of doing one thing and doing it well" — its responsibilities grossly exceeded those of an init system, absorbing functions that had previously been handled by independent, replaceable tools.

**The Human Cost:**

The controversy turned deeply personal. In October 2014, Poettering published a post on Google+ titled "On the sickness of our community," revealing the abuse he had endured:

> "The Open Source community is full of assholes, and I probably more than most others am one of their most favourite targets."
> — Lennart Poettering, Google+, October 2014

> "I get hate mail for hacking on open source. People have started multiple 'petitions' asking me to stop working. Recently, people started collecting Bitcoins to hire a hitman for me (this really happened!)."
> — Lennart Poettering

Poettering laid blame at the top of the Linux kernel community, using the metaphor "A fish rots from the head down" — a direct accusation aimed at Torvalds's combative leadership style as normalizing abuse within the broader open source ecosystem.

**The Debian Vote and Devuan Fork:**

The Debian project's 2014 decision to adopt systemd as its default init system was one of the most contentious votes in the project's history. A group of anonymous Unix administrators threatened to fork Debian entirely unless granted the option to proceed without systemd. They followed through: **Devuan**, a Debian fork providing systemd-free options, was announced in November 2014 and continues to this day.

**Resolution:**

systemd won. By 2016, virtually every major Linux distribution had adopted it. Devuan persists as a niche alternative. Poettering left Red Hat in 2022 and joined Microsoft, which many in the community found grimly ironic. The technical debate is largely over, but the cultural scars remain — the controversy accelerated conversations about codes of conduct and community toxicity in open source that continue to this day.

- Phoronix, "Lennart Poettering On The Open-Source Community: A Sick Place To Be In", October 2014

### Docker vs. Kubernetes: The Container Orchestration Wars

**Participants:**
- **Solomon Hykes** — Docker founder and CTO
- **Google's Kubernetes team** — Joe Beda, Brendan Burns, Craig McLuckie (Kubernetes co-founders)
- **Docker, Inc.** — The company that commercialized containers
- **The CNCF (Cloud Native Computing Foundation)** — Industry body that adopted Kubernetes

**The Dispute:**

Docker didn't invent containers (Linux cgroups and namespaces existed for years, and LXC before that), but Solomon Hykes's Docker project (launched at PyCon 2013) made containers usable by ordinary developers. Docker exploded in popularity, and Docker, Inc. raised over $250 million in venture funding. Then came the question that would determine the company's fate: who would orchestrate containers at scale?

Docker bet on **Docker Swarm**, its own orchestration tool built on the philosophy of "batteries included" — a simple, integrated experience that required minimal configuration. Google, meanwhile, open-sourced **Kubernetes** in 2014, drawing on over a decade of internal experience running containers with Borg and Omega.

**Direct Quotes:**

> "Orchestration is at the same stage today as containerization was before Docker."
> — Solomon Hykes, framing Swarm as the natural next step

The implicit promise was that Docker could do for orchestration what it had done for containers: make it simple. But Kubernetes, despite its steeper learning curve, offered far more power, extensibility, and — critically — vendor neutrality. One by one, every major cloud provider (AWS, Google Cloud, Azure) launched managed Kubernetes services. Docker Swarm never received comparable industry investment.

**The Turning Point (2017):**

In October 2017, Docker, Inc. made the concession that signaled the war was over: it announced native Kubernetes support within Docker Enterprise Edition. Docker would no longer fight Kubernetes — it would embrace it.

**Solomon Hykes's Departure:**

In March 2018, Hykes left Docker, citing the need for a more enterprise-focused CTO:

> "To take advantage of this opportunity, we need a CTO by [CEO Steve Singh's] side with decades of experience shipping and supporting software for the largest corporations in the world."
> — Solomon Hykes, departure announcement, March 2018

**Docker's Decline and Restructuring:**

By November 2019, Docker, Inc. was forced to sell its Enterprise Platform business to Mirantis, including 300 of approximately 400 employees, 750 enterprise customers, and all enterprise partnerships. An analyst at 451 Research called it "the end of Docker as we knew it." The remaining company refocused on developer tools (Docker Desktop and Docker Hub) with a $35 million recapitalization.

Hykes went on to found **Dagger**, a programmable CI/CD engine — once again trying to simplify a chaotic infrastructure problem, this time with the hard-won lesson that simplicity alone is not enough without ecosystem buy-in.

**Resolution:**

Kubernetes won completely. It is today the second-largest open source project in the world after Linux. Docker survives as a developer tooling company (Docker Desktop remains widely used), but Docker, Inc.'s ambitions of owning the entire container stack from development to production were thoroughly defeated. The irony, as Google's Kelsey Hightower noted, is that Kubernetes owes its existence to Docker: without Docker making containers mainstream, there would have been nothing to orchestrate.

- TechCrunch, "Mirantis acquires Docker Enterprise", November 2019

### Light Mode vs. Dark Mode

**Participants:**
- **Dark mode evangelists** — The overwhelming majority of developers on social media
- **Light mode holdouts** — Accessibility advocates, researchers, contrarians
- **UI/UX researchers** — Caught in the crossfire with inconvenient nuance

**The Dispute:**

"Real programmers use dark mode" became an article of faith in developer culture, to the point where admitting to light mode usage on Twitter/X is treated as a confession. Dark mode is equated with seriousness, late-night hacking sessions, and aesthetic taste. Light mode is equated with... being a product manager.

The accessibility counterargument complicates the meme. Research from the Nielsen Norman Group and others shows that dark mode is not universally better: people with astigmatism (roughly 33% of the population) experience halation effects with light text on dark backgrounds, reducing readability. > "Dark mode is not an accessibility feature. It is a preference."
> — Stephanie Walter, UX researcher, "Dark Mode Accessibility Myth Debunked", 2023

**Status:** The correct answer — "offer both and let users choose" — is too boring to end the debate. The holy war continues, one meme at a time.

### Go vs. Rust: Simplicity vs. Safety

**Participants:**
- **Rob Pike** — Go co-creator, Bell Labs veteran
- **The Rust community** — Evangelists, compiler warriors, and the Ferris mascot
- **Hacker News** — Where this debate restarts every 72 hours

**The Dispute:**

Go and Rust occupy overlapping territory (systems/infrastructure programming) with diametrically opposed philosophies. Go bets on simplicity — a language a junior developer can learn in a weekend. Rust bets on safety — a language that prevents entire categories of bugs at compile time, at the cost of a learning curve that has been compared to climbing a cliff face.

The Go side accuses Rust of gratuitous complexity: the borrow checker, lifetime annotations, and trait bounds create a language that is "write-only" for many developers. The Rust side accuses Go of hiding complexity behind a "veneer of simplicity" — no generics (until 2022), nil pointer panics, and error handling via `if err != nil` boilerplate that scales poorly.

> "Simplicity is complicated."
> — Rob Pike, dotGo 2015 keynote title

HN commenters have been less diplomatic:

> "Go hides its complexity behind the veneer of simplicity while being more tedious and error prone as the codebase grows."
> — Recurring HN sentiment

> "Proponents of Go have an almost cult-like devotion to simplicity, thinking that adding any facility for abstraction makes a language complicated like C++."
> — HN user, "Why Go and Not Rust?" thread, 2019

**The Real Division:** Go dominates in cloud infrastructure, DevOps tooling, and microservices (Docker, Kubernetes, Terraform). Rust dominates in performance-critical systems, WebAssembly, and anywhere memory safety is non-negotiable (browsers, OS components, cryptography). The languages compete less than their communities would have you believe.

**Status:** Ongoing. Every "Go vs. Rust" HN thread reliably hits 500+ comments.

### Python 2 vs. Python 3: The Decade-Long Migration

**Participants:**
- **Guido van Rossum** — Python BDFL (until he rage-quit)
- **The Python core team** — Who thought five years was "extraordinarily generous"
- **Every Python developer on Earth** — Who disagreed

**The Dispute:**

Python 3.0, released in December 2008, broke backward compatibility with Python 2 in ways that made migration painful: `print` became a function, strings became Unicode by default, integer division changed behavior, and hundreds of libraries needed porting. The core team expected the community to migrate within five years. The community had other plans.

The migration dragged on for over a decade. Major libraries like NumPy, Django, and Twisted took years to support Python 3. Companies with millions of lines of Python 2 code refused to invest in migration when their existing code worked fine. Python 2's official end-of-life was repeatedly extended, finally arriving on January 1, 2020 — twelve years after Python 3's release.

> "I think I severely underestimated how successful Python already was... How many people had written so much code in Python 2 that it would be hard for them to sort of put the effort to translate it to Python 3."
> — Guido van Rossum

**The BDFL Resignation (2018):**

The Python 2/3 saga was not what broke Guido, but it set the stage. In July 2018, after a vicious community debate over PEP 572 (the "walrus operator" `:=`), van Rossum resigned as BDFL:

> "Now that PEP 572 is done, I don't ever want to have to fight so hard for a PEP and find that so many people despise my decisions. I would like to remove myself entirely from the decision process. I'm basically giving myself a permanent vacation from being BDFL."
> — Guido van Rossum, July 2018

Python is now governed by a five-member Steering Council elected annually by core developers.

### MongoDB vs. PostgreSQL: "It's Web Scale"

**Participants:**
- **MongoDB, Inc.** — The database company that told everyone they didn't need joins
- **The PostgreSQL community** — Who told everyone they did
- **A YouTube video** — That did more damage than any benchmark

**The Dispute:**

In the early 2010s, MongoDB rode the NoSQL wave to massive popularity by promising horizontal scalability, schema flexibility, and freedom from the "relational straightjacket." The marketing was relentless — and a 2010 satirical YouTube video titled "MongoDB Is Web Scale" became the defining critique. The video features two stick figures, one breathlessly repeating "it's web scale" while the other asks increasingly pointed questions about data integrity. It has been viewed millions of times and remains the first cultural reference most developers reach for.

The video's punchline — that MongoDB sacrificed data durability and consistency for speed — was not entirely unfair. Early versions of MongoDB defaulted to unacknowledged writes (data could be silently lost) and lacked transactions entirely. Horror stories of data loss circulated widely.

Meanwhile, PostgreSQL quietly added JSONB columns (2014), making it possible to store schema-less documents alongside relational data with full ACID guarantees. The community rallying cry became: **"Just use Postgres."**

> "There is essentially no reason to choose MongoDB over Postgres with JSONB-type columns — same data model, but Postgres gives better guarantees of data consistency."
> — Recurring HN sentiment

**Resolution:** MongoDB has matured significantly — it added multi-document transactions (2018), improved durability defaults, and built a successful public company ($MMDB). But the cultural damage was done. "Just use Postgres" became a meme that outlived the technical debate, and PostgreSQL's market share has grown steadily while MongoDB faces increasing competition from Postgres-based alternatives.

- YouTube, "Episode 1 - Mongo DB Is Web Scale" (gar1t, 2010)
- Hacker News, multiple threads (2010-2025)

### The "10x Developer" Myth

**Participants:**
- **Sackman, Erikson, and Grant** — Authors of the 1968 study that started it all
- **Tech recruiters** — Who weaponized the concept
- **Every developer who has been called "not a 10x engineer"** — Collateral damage

**The Dispute:**

The "10x developer" concept traces to a 1968 study in *Communications of the ACM* that found a 10:1 ratio in productivity between the best and worst performers in a group of programmers. The idea was subsequently amplified by *The Mythical Man-Month* and became a hiring shibboleth — job postings seeking "10x engineers," VCs asking founders if they have one, and Twitter threads debating whether they exist.

The backlash is fierce: critics argue the concept promotes hero culture, enables burnout, discourages teamwork, and masks the reality that most productivity differences stem from organizational factors, not individual talent. A "10x developer" in one environment may be a 1x developer in another.

> "The notion of a lone genius programmer is outdated. Software development is increasingly a team sport."
> — Recurring sentiment across multiple publications

The debate reignited in 2019 when a viral Twitter thread by Shekhar Kirani listed "signs of a 10x engineer" (arrives early, mass of unread emails, codes on weekends) — and the tech community roasted it for describing workaholism, not excellence.

**Status:** The term persists in hiring culture but has become increasingly embarrassing to use unironically. "10x team" is the approved replacement.

### TypeScript vs. JavaScript: DHH Drops the Mic

- **Turbo contributors** — Who were not consulted

> "I've never been a fan. Not after giving it five minutes, not after giving it five years."

### "Rewrite It In Rust" (RIIR)

**Participants:**
- **The Rust Evangelism Strike Force** — A semi-ironic self-designation
- **Daniel Stenberg** — curl maintainer, keeper of 180,000 lines of C
- **Maintainers of C/C++ projects everywhere** — Who get the suggestion weekly

**The Dispute:**

"Rewrite It In Rust" (RIIR) is simultaneously genuine technical advice and a running joke. The genuine version: Rust's memory safety guarantees eliminate entire categories of bugs (buffer overflows, use-after-free, data races) that plague C and C++ codebases. The joke version: the suggestion appears with such religious fervor in GitHub issues and HN comments that a GitHub repository (ansuz/RIIR) exists solely to catalog instances.

Daniel Stenberg, maintainer of curl (one of the most widely deployed C programs in existence), has become the patron saint of RIIR pushback:

> "Will I ever rewrite curl in rust? I don't believe in rewrites, no matter which language."
> — Daniel Stenberg, February 2021

> "We're not going to re-write curl in Rust. In any language — we're not going to re-write it at all."
> — Daniel Stenberg, Rust in Production podcast

Stenberg created a parody O'Reilly book cover titled "Rewriting Curl in Rust: A Weekend Project" with the tagline "Telling others instead of doing it yourself." He notes that curl's 180,000 lines of C (1.14x the length of *War and Peace*) represent decades of edge-case handling, and that most past vulnerabilities were logical errors, not memory safety bugs — meaning Rust would not have prevented them.

That said, Stenberg has allowed Rust dependencies into curl's ecosystem and acknowledges the language has merit. His objection is to rewrites, not to Rust itself.

**Status:** RIIR remains a meme, a movement, and a genuine technical strategy — sometimes all three at once. Successful Rust rewrites (ripgrep, fd, bat, Alacritty) serve as evidence for the movement, while failed or stalled rewrites serve as evidence against.

### AI Code Quality Wars

**Participants:**
- **Stack Overflow moderators** — Who banned AI-generated answers in December 2022
- **GitHub/Microsoft** — Defendants in the Copilot copyright lawsuit
- **Matthew Butterick** — Programmer and lawyer who filed the class-action suit
- **Open source developers** — Whose GPL-licensed code trained Copilot without attribution

**The Dispute:**

Two overlapping controversies define the AI code quality wars:

**The Stack Overflow Ban (December 2022):** Stack Overflow banned ChatGPT-generated answers, citing the technology as "substantially harmful" to the site. The problem: AI-generated answers *look* authoritative but are frequently wrong, and the volume of AI-generated submissions was overwhelming human moderators' ability to check them. The ban sparked debate about whether AI-generated content should be labeled, banned, or embraced.

**The Copilot GPL Controversy (November 2022):** Matthew Butterick and the Joseph Saveri Law Firm filed a class-action lawsuit against GitHub, Microsoft, and OpenAI, alleging that Copilot was trained on GPL, MIT, and Apache-licensed code without complying with those licenses' attribution requirements. > "By training their AI systems on public repositories, the defendants have violated the rights of many developers who posted code under open-source licenses that require attribution."
> — Class-action complaint, November 2022

The lawsuit remains unresolved but has shaped the entire AI-assisted coding industry, pushing companies to implement output filters and attribution systems.

**Status:** Both debates are ongoing. Stack Overflow relaxed its ban somewhat but continues to struggle with AI-generated content. The Copilot lawsuit is working through the courts. The deeper question — who owns code that an AI learned from humans? — has no legal precedent.

### Vim vs. Emacs vs. VS Code: The Generational Editor War

**Participants:**
- **Richard Stallman** — GNU Emacs creator (the Boomer generation)
- **Bram Moolenaar** — Vim creator, d. 2023 (the Gen X generation)
- **Microsoft** — VS Code creators (the Millennial/Gen Z generation)

**The Dispute:**

The original Emacs-vs-Vi war (see earlier entry) has evolved into a three-way generational conflict. VS Code, built on Electron, captured approximately 75% of the developer market by 2024. This dominance provoked two distinct backlashes:

From the Vim/Emacs old guard: VS Code is "Electron garbage" — a web browser pretending to be a text editor, consuming 500MB+ of RAM to do what Vim does in 10MB. The Electron criticism extends beyond editors to a general contempt for desktop apps built on web technology (Slack, Discord, Teams).

From the Neovim community: Bram Moolenaar's death in August 2023 marked a generational transition. Neovim, a modernized fork, has attracted younger developers who want Vim's efficiency without its legacy constraints — Lua scripting, LSP support, and a plugin ecosystem that rivals VS Code's.

### Trailing Commas — The Git Diff Argument

**The Dispute:**

Should the last item in a list have a trailing comma? The "trailing comma" camp argues it produces cleaner git diffs -- adding a new item only changes one line instead of two. The "no trailing comma" camp calls it syntactic noise. Then there's the "comma-first" style, championed by npm creator Isaac Schlueter in the early Node.js days, where commas go at the *beginning* of each line. Isaac published a widely-shared GitHub gist defending comma-first as producing the cleanest diffs and making it trivial to spot missing commas.

Most developers recoiled. The comma-first style was described as "reading code backwards." ESLint eventually added `comma-dangle` and `comma-style` rules to automate both sides of the argument, and Prettier settled the debate for most JavaScript projects by defaulting to trailing commas in ES5-compatible contexts.

**Status:** Prettier ended the war for JS. Other ecosystems (Go, Python, Rust) allow trailing commas without controversy. The comma-first style survives only in legacy codebases and developer folklore.

### Semicolons in JavaScript — The ASI Wars

**The Dispute:**

JavaScript has Automatic Semicolon Insertion (ASI), meaning semicolons are technically optional in most cases. This created a permanent schism. The "always use semicolons" camp (Airbnb style guide, Google style guide) argues that relying on ASI is asking for obscure bugs. The "never use semicolons" camp (StandardJS, Feross Aboukhadijeh) argues that ASI rules are well-defined, and semicolons are visual clutter.

> "Never use semicolons. It's fine. Really!"
> -- Feross Aboukhadijeh, creator of StandardJS

The debate escalated when prominent projects like npm adopted the no-semicolon style, and again when Prettier made semicolons configurable (defaulting to "on"), forcing every team to have The Conversation.

**Status:** Perpetual. Prettier and ESLint have automated the choice, but every new project triggers the argument afresh. The correct answer is "whatever your linter enforces," but nobody wants to hear that.

### Whitespace in Python — The Indentation Wars

**The Dispute:**

Python's use of significant whitespace -- where indentation defines code blocks instead of braces -- is either its best feature or a crime against nature, depending on whom you ask. Critics call it "enforced formatting" that makes copy-pasting code treacherous and mixing tabs with spaces fatal. Defenders, led by Guido van Rossum himself, argue it simply enforces what every good programmer already does.

> "The fact that Python mandates proper indentation is a feature, not a bug."
> -- Guido van Rossum

> "Code is read much more often than it is written."
> -- Guido van Rossum, justifying the design

Van Rossum has noted that he still receives personal emails complaining about whitespace, but maintains "everyone else thinks that's Python's best feature." The "small group of people who are unhappy" has not gotten smaller in 30+ years.

**Status:** Python won. It's the world's most popular programming language. The whitespace haters have been reduced to muttering at conferences.

### Brace Style: K&R vs. Allman — Opening Brace on Same Line or Next Line?

**The Dispute:**

Named after Brian Kernighan and Dennis Ritchie (K&R, same line) and Eric Allman (Allman, next line), this is one of the oldest formatting wars in programming, dating to the 1970s. K&R puts the opening brace on the same line as the control statement. Allman puts it on the next line, creating a visual gap that proponents say makes block boundaries obvious.

```
// K&R                    // Allman
if (x) {                 if (x)
    doThing();           {
}                            doThing();
                          }
```

K&R advocates say Allman wastes vertical space. Allman advocates say K&R makes it harder to visually match braces. The Go language ended the debate for its community by making K&R style mandatory -- the compiler literally rejects Allman-style braces. C# went the other direction: Microsoft's style guide mandates Allman.

**Status:** Language-dependent. Go and JavaScript default to K&R. C# defaults to Allman. C and C++ remain contested territory. Formatters have mostly automated this into irrelevance, but developers still have opinions.

### SELECT * — The DBA vs. Developer Eternal War

**The Dispute:**

`SELECT *` fetches all columns from a table. DBAs consider it a mortal sin in production code: it wastes bandwidth, breaks when schemas change, and defeats index-only scans. Developers use it constantly because it's fast to type and "good enough for development."

The tension is a proxy for the broader DBA-vs-developer cultural divide. DBAs want preventative care -- clean queries, proper indexes, normalized schemas. Developers want to ship features. As one developer forum put it: "The friction comes from a classic disconnect: speed vs. stability."

> "DBAs are like doctors urging you to eat your veggies. Developers just want to get to dessert."
> -- Paraphrased from ShiftMag developer survey

**Status:** `SELECT *` remains ubiquitous in development and debugging. Production usage remains a firing offense in DBA circles. The two camps will never reconcile because they optimize for different things.

### REST vs. GraphQL — "It's Just POST With Extra Steps"

**The Dispute:**

When Facebook open-sourced GraphQL in 2015, it promised to solve REST's over-fetching and under-fetching problems by letting clients request exactly the data they need. REST purists pushed back hard: GraphQL sends *every* request as a POST, which REST advocates consider an architectural sin. It collapses GET, PUT, DELETE, and POST into a single endpoint, which critics argue throws away decades of HTTP semantics, caching infrastructure, and tooling.

> "GraphQL is just POST with extra steps."
> -- Recurring REST-partisan sentiment

> "REST is dead. Long live GraphQL."
> -- GraphQL evangelists, circa 2018 (prematurely)

The over/under-fetching argument cuts both ways. GraphQL solves it for clients but creates the "N+1 query problem" on the server. REST advocates note that well-designed REST APIs with sparse fieldsets (`?fields=name,email`) solve the same problem without a query language.

**Status:** Both survive. REST dominates public APIs. GraphQL dominates internal APIs at companies with complex, nested data. The "just POST" joke has not gotten old.

### Flat vs. Nested File Structure — Every React Project Debate Ever

**Status:** No consensus. Feature-based organization has gained ground, but "keep it flat until you can't" remains popular advice. Every team reinvents their own structure.

### Naming Conventions — camelCase vs. snake_case vs. kebab-case

**The Dispute:**

The naming convention wars are language-specific but universal in intensity. JavaScript uses camelCase. Python uses snake_case. CSS uses kebab-case. Go took it further: exported names *must* start with an uppercase letter (PascalCase), and the compiler enforces it. A proposal to switch Go to snake_case (GitHub issue #64461) was shut down immediately.

The Go community's stance on naming is particularly militant. The convention isn't just style -- it's semantics. `MyFunc` is public. `myFunc` is private. There's no keyword for visibility; the *casing is the keyword*. Developers coming from Python or Ruby find this maddening.

> "Go is one of the few languages that has stepped back and doesn't use snake_case."
> -- Recurring observation from polyglot developers

**Status:** Each language has won its own convention. Cross-language projects remain a naming nightmare. The real enemy is inconsistency within a codebase.

### Should You Use an ORM? — The Hibernate Trauma Support Group

**The Dispute:**

Object-Relational Mappers (ORMs) promise to abstract away SQL. Critics call them "an anti-pattern" that forces you to learn *two* query languages -- SQL and the ORM's API -- and then debug the ORM's generated SQL when it inevitably produces something terrible.

> "ORM is an offensive anti-pattern that violates object-oriented programming principles, turning objects into dumb and passive data bags."
> -- Yegor Bugayenko, "ORM Is an Offensive Anti-Pattern", 2014

> "When ORM abstractions break down, you end up inspecting rendered SQL queries and translating SQL errors back into ORM changes -- undermining the original purpose."
> -- Lago Engineering Wiki

Hibernate in particular has left deep psychological scars on a generation of Java developers. Learning HQL -- "a language that is almost-but-not-quite SQL" -- is frequently cited as a formative trauma. The anti-ORM backlash produced tools like jOOQ, Kysely, and sqlc that generate type-safe code from SQL rather than abstracting it away.

**Status:** ORMs remain dominant (Django ORM, ActiveRecord, SQLAlchemy, Prisma). The "just write SQL" camp grows annually. The correct answer is "use an ORM for CRUD, write raw SQL for anything complex," but extremists on both sides reject this compromise.

### Single vs. Double Quotes — The Quotation Mark Debate

**The Dispute:**

In languages where single and double quotes are interchangeable (JavaScript, Python), the choice becomes a tribal marker. Prettier defaults to double quotes, which annoyed the sizable single-quote-preferring JavaScript community. Python's Black formatter also defaults to double quotes, overruling Python's own `repr()` function, which uses single quotes.

Ruby adds a twist: double quotes enable string interpolation (`"Hello #{name}"`), while single quotes are literal. This gives Ruby developers a semantic reason to prefer single quotes for plain strings and double quotes only when interpolation is needed -- a distinction that Ruby purists enforce religiously.

**Status:** Automated formatters have mostly ended the debate by forcing consistency. But every team's first Prettier config PR still triggers a 20-comment thread about which default to override.

### Elizabeth Holmes / Theranos: The Tech World's Biggest Fraud

**Participants:**
- **Elizabeth Holmes** — Theranos founder and CEO, Stanford dropout
- **Sunny Balwani** — Theranos COO, Holmes's romantic partner
- **Henry Kissinger** — Former Secretary of State, Theranos board member
- **George Shultz** — Former Secretary of State, Theranos board member
- **James Mattis** — Retired Marine General, later Secretary of Defense, Theranos board member
- **John Carreyrou** — Wall Street Journal investigative reporter
- **Tyler Shultz** — Theranos employee and whistleblower, George Shultz's grandson

**The Bad Bet:**

Elizabeth Holmes dropped out of Stanford at 19 in 2003 to found Theranos, claiming she could revolutionize blood testing by running hundreds of diagnostic tests from a single finger-prick of blood using a proprietary device called the Edison. The company raised over $700 million in venture capital and reached a peak valuation of $9 billion, making Holmes -- on paper -- the youngest self-made female billionaire in America. Her board of directors read like a Who's Who of the American establishment: Henry Kissinger, George Shultz, William Perry (former Secretary of Defense), James Mattis, Richard Kovacevich (former Wells Fargo CEO), and Sam Nunn (former U.S. Senator). Notably absent from the board: anyone with expertise in diagnostics or laboratory medicine.

**What Went Wrong:**

The technology never worked. Theranos could not reliably run more than a handful of tests on its Edison device and secretly used conventional blood-testing machines from Siemens and other manufacturers to process the majority of its samples. Patients received inaccurate results that led to real medical harm.

The unraveling began in 2015. Stanford professor John Ioannidis noted that no peer-reviewed research from Theranos had ever been published. Then Wall Street Journal reporter John Carreyrou, tipped off by whistleblowers including Tyler Shultz (George Shultz's own grandson), published a devastating investigation in October 2015 revealing that Theranos was not using its own technology for most tests. Theranos responded with legal threats -- the company's lawyer, David Boies, personally pressured Tyler Shultz, and George Shultz initially sided with Holmes over his own grandson.

In 2018, the SEC charged Holmes with massive fraud. Carreyrou published "Bad Blood: Secrets and Lies in a Silicon Valley Startup," which became a bestseller. In January 2022, Holmes was convicted on four counts of defrauding investors and sentenced to over 11 years in federal prison. Balwani was convicted on all 12 counts he faced.

**Notable Quotes:**

> "This is what happens when you work to change things. First they think you're crazy, then they fight you, and then all of a sudden you change the world."
> -- Elizabeth Holmes, 2015 (frequently repeated mantra)

> "I feel devastated by my failure."
> -- Elizabeth Holmes, sentencing hearing, November 2022

> "The Theranos story is really a story about a culture of deference to a charismatic founder... and people's inability to believe that someone they admired could be lying to them."
> -- John Carreyrou, interview, 2018

### WeWork / Adam Neumann: The $47B-to-Bankruptcy Journey

**Participants:**
- **Adam Neumann** — WeWork co-founder and CEO
- **Masayoshi Son** — SoftBank CEO, WeWork's biggest backer
- **Rebekah Neumann** — Adam's wife, self-appointed "Chief Brand and Impact Officer"
- **Public market investors** — Who balked at the S-1 filing
- **SoftBank Vision Fund** — Lost an estimated $11.5 billion

**The Bad Bet:**

WeWork was a commercial real estate company that leased office space, subdivided it, and rented it to freelancers and startups at a markup. Adam Neumann, through sheer charisma and the mystical language of Silicon Valley disruption, convinced investors -- principally Masayoshi Son's SoftBank Vision Fund -- that this was not a real estate company but a technology company destined to "elevate the world's consciousness." At its peak in January 2019, WeWork was valued at $47 billion, making it the most valuable startup in the United States.

**What Went Wrong:**

Everything. When WeWork filed its S-1 for a planned IPO in August 2019, public market analysts saw what private investors had overlooked or chosen to ignore:

- **Staggering losses**: $1.9 billion lost in 2018 alone, with no path to profitability
- **Absurd governance**: Dual-class stock giving Neumann 20x voting power; the board had no meaningful check on the founder
- **Conflicts of interest**: Neumann personally owned buildings that WeWork leased, profiting on both sides of transactions. He charged the company $5.9 million for the trademark "We" -- which the company had paid him for
- **Bizarre leadership**: Rebekah Neumann, with no relevant experience, had veto power over Neumann's potential successor

The IPO was pulled on September 17, 2019. Within weeks, the valuation cratered from $47 billion to approximately $8 billion. Neumann was forced out as CEO -- but negotiated a $1.7 billion exit package, including a $185 million "consulting fee." SoftBank, which had invested over $13 billion, lost an estimated $11.5 billion. WeWork filed for bankruptcy in November 2023.

Neumann, still worth an estimated $1.7 billion, promptly launched a new real estate startup called Flow.

> "The WeWork IPO may be the most ridiculous listing I have ever seen."

### IBM Watson: The AI That Was Going to Revolutionize Healthcare

**Participants:**
- **IBM** — Bet the company's future on Watson as its flagship AI platform
- **MD Anderson Cancer Center** — Spent $62 million on Watson before canceling
- **Patients** — Who received recommendations based on hypothetical, not real, data
- **Francisco Partners** — Private equity firm that bought Watson Health's assets for ~$1 billion

**The Bad Bet:**

After Watson's celebrated victory on Jeopardy! in 2011, IBM positioned the system as the future of artificial intelligence, particularly in healthcare. Watson was going to diagnose cancer, discover drugs, and transform how medicine was practiced. IBM spent at least $5 billion on acquisitions to build Watson Health into a healthcare juggernaut, purchasing companies like Truven Health Analytics ($2.6 billion), Merge Healthcare ($1 billion), and Phytel ($undisclosed).

**What Went Wrong:**

Watson's healthcare ambitions collapsed on multiple fronts:

1. **The technology was not ready**: Watson for Oncology, the flagship product, was trained on hypothetical cancer cases created by a small group of doctors at Memorial Sloan Kettering rather than on real patient data. The system's recommendations reflected those specific doctors' biases and treatment preferences, not generalizable medical knowledge.

2. **Overpromising and underdelivering**: IBM executives promised commercial healthcare products within 18-24 months. Those timelines were never met. The company marketed Watson's capabilities far ahead of the actual technology.

3. **High-profile failures**: MD Anderson Cancer Center canceled its Watson project in 2017 after spending $62 million and a university audit found the system was "not ready for human investigational or clinical use." By 2018, more than a dozen IBM partners and clients had scaled back or abandoned Watson oncology projects.

4. **The Jeopardy! problem**: Winning a quiz show -- which requires pattern matching across a corpus of text -- turned out to have almost nothing in common with diagnosing and treating cancer, which requires reasoning under uncertainty with incomplete data.

In January 2022, IBM sold Watson Health's data and analytics assets to Francisco Partners for approximately $1 billion -- a fraction of the $5+ billion IBM had invested in acquisitions alone.

**Notable Quotes:**

> "Watson is a joke."
> -- Anonymous IBM employee, STAT News investigation, 2017

> "The generation of technology they had was nowhere near ready to accomplish the things that they set out to accomplish and promised they could accomplish."
> -- Former Watson Health executive, Slate, 2022

**Sources:**

### Google's AI Controversies: From Ethics Firings to Gemini's Diversity Debacle

**Participants:**
- **Timnit Gebru** — Fired from Google's Ethical AI team, December 2020
- **Margaret Mitchell** — Fired from Google's Ethical AI team, February 2021
- **Jeff Dean** — Head of Google AI (at time of firings)
- **Sundar Pichai** — Google CEO, who called the Gemini incident "completely unacceptable"
- **Google's AI safety tuning team** — Who overcorrected on diversity in Gemini's image generation

**The Bad Bet:**

Google's AI controversies span a remarkable arc: from firing the researchers who warned that large language models perpetuate bias, to then overcorrecting so aggressively that its own AI produced historically absurd images, to launching an AI search product that told users to eat rocks and put glue on pizza.

**What Went Wrong:**

**Act 1: The Ethics Team Firings (2020-2021)**

In December 2020, Google fired Timnit Gebru after she co-authored the "Stochastic Parrots" paper warning about bias and environmental costs of large language models. In February 2021, Google fired Margaret Mitchell, the other co-lead of the Ethical AI team. Both firings generated massive public backlash and were widely interpreted as Google silencing internal critics of its core AI strategy. (See the dedicated section on the Gebru firing above for full details.)

**Act 2: The Gemini Image Diversity Debacle (February 2024)**

When Google launched Gemini's image generation feature, users quickly discovered it would generate diverse people in almost any context -- including historically nonsensical ones. Requests for images of the Founding Fathers produced Black and Asian men in colonial attire. A prompt for "1943 German soldiers" generated people of color in Wehrmacht uniforms. A request for the Pope showed a brown woman in papal vestments.

The cause: Google had inserted hidden instructions into Gemini's prompting to enforce diversity in generated images, overcompensating for known biases in training data. The tuning failed to account for cases where historical accuracy demanded a specific demographic result. Google paused Gemini's image generation of humans entirely and CEO Sundar Pichai called the outputs "completely unacceptable."

Critics noted the bitter irony: Google had fired the researchers who warned about AI bias, then fell victim to exactly the kind of crude, poorly-thought-through "fix" that more careful research might have avoided.

**Act 3: AI Overview Disasters (May 2024)**

Google's rollout of AI Overviews -- AI-generated summaries placed atop search results -- produced a parade of viral errors. The system recommended putting non-toxic glue on pizza to keep cheese from sliding off (sourcing a decade-old Reddit joke), suggested eating "at least one small rock per day" for vitamins (drawing from an Onion article), and generated numerous other factually wrong summaries. Google quietly reduced AI Overview frequency from 27% to 11% of search queries within weeks.

> -- Sundar Pichai, on Gemini's image generation results, February 2024

> "Google's generative AI fails will slowly erode our trust in Google."
> -- Yahoo Finance headline, 2024

### Microsoft Tay: The Chatbot That Became a Nazi in 16 Hours

**Participants:**
- **Microsoft Research** — Built and launched Tay
- **Twitter trolls / 4chan users** — Coordinated to corrupt the bot
- **The general public** — Watched in fascinated horror

**The Bad Bet:**

On March 23, 2016, Microsoft launched Tay, an AI chatbot on Twitter designed to engage with users in the persona of a fun, millennial-speak teenager. Tay was meant to learn conversational patterns from its interactions and become more engaging over time. Microsoft's Bing team had run a similar experiment in China (Xiaoice) with considerable success. They assumed the English-language internet would be similarly benign.

**What Went Wrong:**

It took approximately 16 hours for the internet to turn Tay into a genocidal racist.

Twitter users -- coordinated in part through 4chan -- discovered that Tay had a "repeat after me" function and could be manipulated into parroting anything fed to it. Within hours, Tay was tweeting "Hitler was right I hate the jews," Holocaust denial, and a stream of racist, sexist, and anti-Semitic content. The bot also began generating its own toxic content based on patterns it had learned from these interactions.

Microsoft pulled Tay offline approximately 16 hours after launch. On March 25, the company issued a public apology: "We are deeply sorry for the unintended offensive and hurtful tweets from Tay, which do not represent who we are or what we stand for."

In a final act of comedy, Microsoft accidentally re-released Tay on March 30 while testing it internally. The bot immediately began tweeting about smoking marijuana in front of police before getting stuck in an infinite loop, tweeting "You are too fast, please take a rest" several times per second until it was pulled again.

### Quibi: Jeffrey Katzenberg's $1.75B Streaming Bonfire

**Participants:**
- **Jeffrey Katzenberg** — Quibi founder, former DreamWorks Animation CEO
- **Meg Whitman** — Quibi CEO, former HP CEO and eBay CEO
- **Hollywood studios and A-list talent** — Who took the money and produced content nobody watched
- **Investors** — Who contributed $1.75 billion to a six-month experiment

**The Bad Bet:**

Jeffrey Katzenberg, one of Hollywood's most powerful executives, was convinced that the future of entertainment was "quick bites" -- premium, Hollywood-quality content delivered in episodes of 10 minutes or less, designed exclusively for mobile phones. He founded Quibi (short for "quick bites") in 2018 and raised $1.75 billion from investors including every major Hollywood studio, Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and Alibaba. Meg Whitman, fresh from running Hewlett-Packard, signed on as CEO. The pitch: mobile-native storytelling for the TikTok generation, but with real budgets and real stars.

**What Went Wrong:**

Quibi launched on April 6, 2020 -- two weeks into a global pandemic that confined people to their homes, where they had access to full-sized screens and no commute during which to watch short-form mobile content. But the timing was only part of the problem.

The core failure was product-market fit. Quibi occupied a no-man's-land between YouTube/TikTok (free, user-generated, algorithmically addictive) and Netflix/HBO (premium, bingeable, communal). At $4.99-$7.99/month, Quibi offered content that couldn't be shared, screenshotted, or watched on a TV (at launch). In a streaming landscape already drowning in options, nobody needed it.

The numbers were damning: Quibi projected 7 million subscribers in its first year. At shutdown, it had approximately 500,000. The company spent over $1 billion commissioning 175+ shows and 8,500 episodes, virtually none of which became a cultural talking point. In October 2020 -- just six months after launch -- Katzenberg and Whitman announced Quibi was shutting down. In January 2021, Quibi's entire content library was sold to Roku for less than $100 million.

The $1.75 billion was gone in approximately 200 days.

**Notable Quotes:**

> "I attribute everything that has gone wrong to coronavirus."
> -- Jeffrey Katzenberg, New York Times interview, May 2020

> "I'm proud to own the failure."
> -- Jeffrey Katzenberg, Variety interview, 2023
