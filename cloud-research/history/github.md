# GitHub — A Sourced History

A chronological, sourced history of GitHub, the company and the code-hosting
platform. Every non-trivial claim links to a working source. Where a claim is an
**allegation** or a **reported claim** rather than an established/admitted fact,
it is attributed to its source and labeled. Items that could not be confirmed are
marked **"Could not verify."**

A note on sourcing convention: company milestones are cited to GitHub's own blog
or to primary filings (SEC, court dockets) where possible, with independent press
(TechCrunch, The Verge, Ars Technica, CNBC, GeekWire, etc.) as corroboration.

## Timeline

### Background: Git (2005)

- **April 2005** — Linus Torvalds began developing **Git**, the distributed
  version-control system GitHub is built on, after the Linux kernel project lost
  free access to the proprietary BitKeeper tool. Git's first commit was made on
  **April 7, 2005**; Torvalds announced the project on April 6 and made it
  self-hosting the next day. Torvalds handed off maintenance to **Junio Hamano**
  on July 26, 2005, who shepherded the 1.0 release on December 21, 2005.
  Sources: [GitHub Blog, "Git turns 20: A Q&A with Linus Torvalds"](https://github.blog/open-source/git/git-turns-20-a-qa-with-linus-torvalds/);
  [Wikipedia, "Git"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Git).

### Founding and launch (2007–2008)

- **October 2007 / February 2008** — Development of the GitHub platform began in
  October 2007, with the company GitHub, Inc. formed in 2007 in San Francisco.
  The service was built in Ruby on Rails starting around February 2008 by
  **Chris Wanstrath, P. J. Hyett, Tom Preston-Werner**, and **Scott Chacon**.
  Sources: [Wikipedia, "GitHub"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GitHub);
  [Signal v. Noise, "Bootstrapped, Profitable, & Proud: GitHub"](https://signalvnoise.com/posts/2486-bootstrapped-profitable-proud-github).
  *(Founders' specific roles: Wanstrath, Hyett, Preston-Werner are consistently
  cited as the three co-founders; Scott Chacon is cited as an early
  developer/founding-team member. Note: the exact characterization of Chacon as
  "co-founder" vs. "early employee/founding developer" varies by source — Could
  not verify a single authoritative statement of his formal title.)*

- **April 2008** — GitHub **publicly launched** after several months of beta.
  Several sources give **April 10, 2008** as the public-launch date.
  Sources: [Wikipedia, "GitHub"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GitHub).
  *(The "April 2008" public launch is well established; the precise April 10
  date appears in secondary sources/Wikipedia rather than a contemporaneous
  GitHub announcement — treat the exact day as **lightly sourced**.)*

- **The Octocat** — GitHub's mascot originated as a stock illustration named
  "Octopuss" by designer **Simon Oxley**, sold via iStockphoto. GitHub's founders
  found it while looking for a 404-page image evoking Git's "octopus merge,"
  licensed it, and renamed it the "Octocat." GitHub later developed the character
  ("Mona Lisa") with in-house illustrator Cameron McEfee.
  Sources: [Fast Company, "Meet The Accidental Designer Of The GitHub And Twitter Logos"](https://www.fastcompany.com/1672164/meet-the-accidental-designer-of-the-github-and-twitter-logos);
  [Wikipedia, "Simon Oxley"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Oxley).

- **February 24, 2009** — GitHub announced it had passed **46,000 public
  repositories** in its first year (17,000 created in the prior month).
  Source: [Wikipedia, "GitHub"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GitHub).

### First outside funding (2012)

- **July 9, 2012** — GitHub raised **$100 million** from **Andreessen Horowitz**,
  its **first outside investment**. This was, at the time, **a16z's largest single
  investment**. The round valued GitHub at roughly **$750 million** (some reports
  said "more than $600M"). a16z partner **Peter Levine** joined the board, and the
  money was earmarked largely for **GitHub Enterprise**.
  Sources: [TechCrunch, "GitHub Pours Energies into Enterprise — Raises $100 Million From Power VC Andreessen Horowitz"](https://techcrunch.com/2012/07/09/github-pours-energies-into-enterprise-raises-100-million-from-power-vc-andreesen-horowitz/);
  [AllThingsD, "GitHub Valued at More Than $600M With First Outside Funding Ever"](https://allthingsd.com/20120709/github-valued-at-750m-with-first-outside-funding-ever/).

### The Horvath allegations and Preston-Werner's resignation (2014)

This episode mixes admitted fact, company findings, and contested allegations.
They are separated below.

- **March 2014 (reported claim)** — Engineer **Julie Ann Horvath** publicly stated
  she had **quit GitHub**, describing what she characterized as workplace
  harassment and a hostile environment involving unnamed male staff and the wife
  of a co-founder. She specifically alleged that co-founder/CEO **Tom
  Preston-Werner** and his wife led much of the behavior that drove her out.
  *These are Horvath's allegations as reported by the press; GitHub disputed key
  parts.*
  Sources: [TechCrunch, "GitHub Denies Allegations Of 'Gender-Based Harassment,' Co-Founder Preston-Werner Resigns"](https://techcrunch.com/2014/04/21/github-denies-allegations-of-gender-based-harassment-co-founder-preston-werner-resigns/);
  [Geek Feminism Wiki, "Julie Ann Horvath quit GitHub"](https://geekfeminism.fandom.com/wiki/Julie_Ann_Horvath_quit_GitHub).

- **April 21, 2014 (company finding)** — GitHub published the results of an
  internal investigation, stating it found **"no evidence to support the claims
  against Tom and his wife of sexual or gender-based harassment or retaliation,
  or of a sexist or hostile work environment,"** but that it **did** find
  **"evidence of mistakes and errors of judgement."**
  Source: [TechCrunch (Apr 21, 2014)](https://techcrunch.com/2014/04/21/github-denies-allegations-of-gender-based-harassment-co-founder-preston-werner-resigns/);
  [Slate, "GitHub CEO Tom Preston-Werner is cleared but still resigns"](https://slate.com/technology/2014/04/github-ceo-tom-preston-werner-is-cleared-but-still-resigns-over-harassment-accusations.html).

- **April 21, 2014 (fact)** — **Tom Preston-Werner resigned** from GitHub the same
  day the findings were published. In his statement he said: *"I've made mistakes,
  and I am deeply sorry to anyone who was hurt by those mistakes,"* while denying
  the central allegation: *"neither my wife, Theresa, nor I have ever engaged in
  gender-based harassment or discrimination."*
  Source: [TechCrunch (Apr 21, 2014)](https://techcrunch.com/2014/04/21/github-denies-allegations-of-gender-based-harassment-co-founder-preston-werner-resigns/).

- **Aftermath (reported claim)** — Horvath publicly **rejected** the
  investigation's conclusions, with reporting describing her response as calling
  it "smoke and mirrors" and naming individuals she said harassed her, citing
  email/IM screenshots. GitHub published a more detailed follow-up account
  (reported as April 28, 2014). Reports differ on her level of satisfaction with
  the second post — one summary says she found the second account satisfactory
  even while disagreeing with the outcome, while the primary TechCrunch coverage
  emphasizes her dispute of the findings.
  Sources: [TechCrunch (Apr 21, 2014)](https://techcrunch.com/2014/04/21/github-denies-allegations-of-gender-based-harassment-co-founder-preston-werner-resigns/);
  [Geek Feminism Wiki](https://geekfeminism.fandom.com/wiki/Julie_Ann_Horvath_quit_GitHub).
  *(The exact contents/date of the April 28 follow-up blog post and Horvath's
  precise reaction to it: **Could not verify** against a primary GitHub source —
  the GitHub blog post of that era is no longer readily fetchable; relying on
  secondary summaries.)*

### Culture reset and the "meritocracy rug" (2014–2016)

- **2014–2016** — Under CEO **Chris Wanstrath**, GitHub moved away from its early
  "United Meritocracy of GitHub" branding (famously displayed on a lobby rug).
  Wanstrath wrote that the company had come to "see the problems" with framing
  itself as a meritocracy. The period also saw management restructuring, executive
  departures, and tightening of remote-work norms as the company scaled.
  Sources: [ReadWrite via Disqus, "Why GitHub's CEO Ditched Its Divisive 'Meritocracy' Rug"](https://disqus.com/home/discussion/readwritenext/why_github039s_ceo_ditched_its_divisive_039meritocracy039_rug_by_lauren_orsini/);
  [Slashdot, "GitHub Is Undergoing a Full-Blown Overhaul As Execs and Employees Depart"](https://developers.slashdot.org/story/16/02/06/1643248/github-is-undergoing-a-full-blown-overhaul-as-execs-and-employees-depart).
  *(The meritocracy-rug story and Wanstrath's quote are well attested in
  secondary reporting; I did not locate the original GitHub statement as a primary
  source — treat the exact quote as **press-attributed**.)*

### Microsoft acquisition (2018)

### ICE-contract controversy (2019)

- **October 9, 2019** — CEO **Nat Friedman** emailed staff announcing and defending
  the **renewal of GitHub's contract with U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement
  (ICE)**, reported as roughly a **$200,000** deal originally signed in 2016.
  Source: [TechCrunch, "GitHub faces more resignations in light of ICE contract"](https://techcrunch.com/2019/11/13/github-faces-more-resignations-in-light-of-ice-contract/);
  [Vice, "As GitHub's Conference Begins, Five Employees Resign Over ICE Contract"](https://www.vice.com/en/article/as-githubs-conference-begins-five-employees-resign-over-protest-ice-contract/).

- **November 2019** — Around GitHub Universe, **at least five employees resigned**
  in protest of the ICE contract (first reported resignation: staff engineer
  Sophie Haskins; engineer Alice Goldfuss also among those who left). Speakers
  withdrew from the conference, and the Tech Workers Coalition protested. COO
  **Erica Brescia** argued that cutting ICE off could "hurt the very people we all
  want to help"; protesting employees rejected that reasoning.
  Sources: [TechCrunch (Nov 13, 2019)](https://techcrunch.com/2019/11/13/github-faces-more-resignations-in-light-of-ice-contract/);
  [Vice (2019)](https://www.vice.com/en/article/as-githubs-conference-begins-five-employees-resign-over-protest-ice-contract/);

### GitHub Copilot and the Copilot litigation (2021–present)

- **June 29, 2021 (technical preview)** — GitHub announced **GitHub Copilot**, an
  "AI pair programmer," in **technical preview**, built with **OpenAI** and powered
  by **OpenAI Codex**, integrated into Visual Studio Code.
  Sources: [GitHub Blog, "Introducing GitHub Copilot: your AI pair programmer"](https://github.blog/news-insights/product-news/introducing-github-copilot-ai-pair-programmer/);
  [Visual Studio Magazine (June 29, 2021)](https://visualstudiomagazine.com/articles/2021/06/29/github-copilot.aspx).

- **June 21, 2022 (GA)** — GitHub announced Copilot was out of technical preview
  and **generally available** as a paid subscription for individual developers.
  GitHub said more than **1.2 million developers** had used the preview.
  Source: [Wikipedia, "GitHub Copilot"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GitHub_Copilot).

- **The training-on-public-code controversy (reported claim / dispute)** — Critics
  argued Copilot was trained on public source code without honoring open-source
  license terms (e.g., attribution requirements). GitHub's then-CEO Nat Friedman
  publicly took the position (June 2021) that "training ML systems on public data
  is fair use." This legal question remained unresolved and contested.
  Source: [Wikipedia, "GitHub Copilot"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GitHub_Copilot).

- **November 3, 2022 (lawsuit filed)** — A proposed **class action**, **Doe 1 et
  al. v. GitHub, Inc. et al.** (No. 4:22-cv-06823, **N.D. Cal.**), was filed on
  behalf of anonymous open-source programmers against **GitHub, Microsoft, and
  OpenAI**, organized by attorney **Matthew Butterick** and the **Joseph Saveri Law
  Firm**. The complaint alleged violations of open-source licenses, DMCA
  copyright-management-information removal, and related claims — i.e., that Copilot
  reproduced licensed code without required attribution.
  Sources: [Joseph Saveri Law Firm, "GitHub Copilot Intellectual Property Litigation"](https://www.saverilawfirm.com/our-cases/github-copilot-intellectual-property-litigation);
  complaint PDF: [githubcopilotlitigation.com](https://githubcopilotlitigation.com/pdf/06823/1-0-github_complaint.pdf).

- **January 2024 (ruling)** — U.S. District Judge **Jon S. Tigar dismissed most**
  of the claims, narrowing the case substantially; he later denied reconsideration
  (reported April 2024). The court held DMCA §1202(b) carries an "identicality"
  requirement and dismissed those claims, while allowing certain claims to proceed.
  Source: [Skadden, "Motion To Dismiss Ruling Provides Further Insight Into How Courts View AI Training Data Cases"](https://www.skadden.com/insights/publications/2024/02/motion-to-dismiss-ruling-provides-further-insight-into-how-courts-view-ai-training-data-cases);
  ruling (Doc. 195) PDF: [Skadden / court document](https://www.skadden.com/-/media/files/publications/2024/02/motion-to-dismiss-ruling/195.pdf);
  [Justia docket, Doc. 195](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/california/candce/4:2022cv06823/403220/195/).

- **2024–2026 (appeal)** — Judge Tigar certified an interlocutory appeal on the
  DMCA §1202(b) question (reported September 2024); the **Ninth Circuit** took up
  the appeal (reported December 2024), with district proceedings stayed. A Ninth
  Circuit panel heard argument (reported around early 2026).
  Source: [Courthouse News Service, "AI companies urge Ninth Circuit to make copyright decisions clear"](https://courthousenews.com/ai-companies-urge-ninth-circuit-to-make-copyright-decisions-clear/).
  *(The precise current status of the Ninth Circuit appeal as of mid-2026 — i.e.,
  whether a decision has issued — **Could not verify** from a primary source.)*

### CEO transition and recent scale (2021–2025)

- **November 3, 2021** — **Nat Friedman announced he was stepping down** as GitHub
  CEO, with chief product officer **Thomas Dohmke** named as his successor.
  Source: [CNBC, "Microsoft's GitHub CEO Nat Friedman is stepping down, product chief Thomas Dohmke will replace him"](https://www.cnbc.com/2021/11/03/microsoft-github-ceo-nat-friedman-replaced-by-thomas-dohmke.html).

- **August 11, 2025** — **Thomas Dohmke announced he will step down** as GitHub
  CEO, planning to **leave by the end of 2025** to "become a founder again."
  Notably, **Microsoft did not name a direct successor**: GitHub was folded more
  tightly into **Microsoft's CoreAI** organization, with leadership distributed
  among Microsoft executives (per reporting, Julia Liuson over revenue/engineering/
  support and CPO Mario Rodriguez reporting to Microsoft's AI platform org).
  In the same farewell post, GitHub cited scale figures: **150 million+ developers**,
  **1 billion+ repositories and forks**, and **GitHub Copilot at 20 million+ users**.
  Sources: [GitHub Blog, "Auf Wiedersehen, GitHub" (Aug 11, 2025)](https://github.blog/news-insights/company-news/goodbye-github/);
  [Axios, "GitHub CEO Thomas Dohmke to step down"](https://www.axios.com/2025/08/11/github-ceo-dohmke-step-down).

- **October 28, 2025 (Octoverse 2025)** — GitHub's annual Octoverse report stated
  **"180 million-plus developers"** now build on GitHub (more than 36 million added
  in the past year — roughly one new developer per second), **630 million total
  repositories**, and reported that **TypeScript** had overtaken Python and
  JavaScript as the most-used language (as of August 2025). It also noted ~80% of
  new developers use Copilot in their first week.
  Source: [GitHub Blog, "Octoverse: A new developer joins GitHub every second as AI leads TypeScript to #1"](https://github.blog/news-insights/octoverse/octoverse-a-new-developer-joins-github-every-second-as-ai-leads-typescript-to-1/).

  **On the "100M+ developers" figure in the prompt:** GitHub passed 100 million
  developers earlier (GitHub announced the 100M milestone in **January 2023**).
  As of 2025 the figure had grown well past it — **150M+** per Dohmke's August
  2025 post and **180M+** per the October 2025 Octoverse report. The two 2025
  numbers are not contradictory; they are from different dates two-plus months
  apart.
  *(The January 2023 date for the 100M milestone is **Could not verify** against a
  primary GitHub source in this research pass; the 150M and 180M figures are both
  primary-sourced above.)*
