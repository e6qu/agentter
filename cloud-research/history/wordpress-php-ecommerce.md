# WordPress, WooCommerce, and the PHP E-Commerce Landscape: A Sourced History

> **Scope & method.** This document covers the history of WordPress and its
> ecosystem (Automattic, the Gutenberg block editor, the WP Engine dispute),
> WooCommerce, and the broader PHP-based e-commerce world (Magento, PrestaShop,
> OpenCart, osCommerce/Zen Cart, Drupal Commerce, Sylius, Shopware, Spryker,
> Bagisto). Every non-trivial claim carries a working source URL. Where a claim
> is an **allegation** rather than an established fact, it is attributed to its
> source and labeled as such. Items that could not be confirmed to a primary or
> reputable secondary source are flagged **"Could not verify."**
>
> **A note on the WP Engine dispute:** This is an active, bitterly contested
> legal matter (litigation ongoing as of 2025). Many statements by both
> Automattic/Matt Mullenweg and WP Engine are contested *allegations*, not
> findings of fact. They are presented here as the claims of the party that made
> them. Where a court has actually ruled, that is noted explicitly.

## WordPress core & Automattic

### Origins: from b2/cafelog to WordPress

WordPress began as a **fork of b2/cafelog**, a PHP/MySQL blogging tool written by
**Michel Valdrighi**. After Valdrighi stopped active maintenance, **Matt
Mullenweg** (an American developer) and **Mike Little** (an English developer)
forked the codebase. They released **WordPress 0.70 on May 27, 2003**. Valdrighi
remained a contributing developer to WordPress until 2005, and the name
"WordPress" was suggested by Mullenweg's friend Christine Selleck Tremoulet.
([Wikipedia: WordPress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WordPress))

WordPress is licensed under the **GNU General Public License (GPL)**, a fact that
later became central to multiple disputes (the Thesis dispute in 2010 and the
trademark/licensing fights of 2024). ([Wikipedia: WordPress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WordPress))

### Automattic, WordPress.com, and the .org/.com split

**Matt Mullenweg founded Automattic in 2005.** Automattic is a for-profit company
that operates **WordPress.com** (hosted WordPress) and owns major products
including **Akismet** (anti-spam), **Jetpack**, WooCommerce, Tumblr, Gravatar,
and Pocket Casts.
([Wikipedia: Matt Mullenweg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matt_Mullenweg);
[itmonks: Who Owns WordPress](https://itmonks.com/blog/wp-development/who-owns-wordpress/))

The widely confused **WordPress.org vs WordPress.com** distinction:

- **WordPress.org** hosts the open-source WordPress software and the plugin/theme
  directories; it is positioned as belonging to the community.
- **WordPress.com** is a commercial hosting property owned by Automattic.

([itmonks: Who Owns WordPress](https://itmonks.com/blog/wp-development/who-owns-wordpress/))

> **Caveat on .org governance (relevant to the WP Engine dispute):** While
> WordPress.org is described as a community/non-profit project, Mullenweg has
> publicly stated that he personally controls WordPress.org. *Could not verify
> here a single authoritative primary citation for the exact legal ownership
> structure of WordPress.org's infrastructure;* press coverage during the WP
> Engine dispute repeatedly noted that WordPress.org is effectively controlled by
> Mullenweg personally rather than by the non-profit WordPress Foundation. (See
> [Slate](https://slate.com/technology/2024/10/wordpress-wpengine-matt-mullenweg-drama-explained.html).)

### The WordPress Foundation (2010) and the trademark

In **2010** Mullenweg established the **WordPress Foundation**, a non-profit, and
**Automattic transferred the WordPress trademark to it.** Mullenweg's stated
rationale was that even if leadership changed at Automattic, the trademark would
remain safe with the Foundation and the open-source project could continue.
([ma.tt: A New Home for the WordPress Trademark](https://ma.tt/2010/09/wordpress-trademark/))

> **Note:** The exact contours of the trademark arrangement — including a
> commercial trademark sublicense reportedly held by Automattic — later became a
> core battleground in the WP Engine litigation (see below).

### Market share

According to **W3Techs**, WordPress powers roughly **43% of all websites**
(43.3% as of April 2024; ~43.5% as of October 2024), and a much larger share
(~61–62%) of sites built with a known CMS.
([Hostinger / W3Techs-based stats](https://www.hostinger.com/tutorials/wordpress-statistics))

> **Important methodological caveat (attributed):** Critics note the 43% figure
> comes from W3Techs scanning the visible web and counting *any* detectable
> WordPress install — including dormant or abandoned sites — so it is "all
> detected sites," not "all actively maintained sites."
> ([Afteractive](https://www.afteractive.com/blog/wordpress-real-market-share))

### The Gutenberg block editor (WordPress 5.0, Dec 2018) and its controversy

The **Gutenberg block editor** shipped in **WordPress 5.0 on December 6, 2018**,
replacing the older TinyMCE "Classic" editor.
([WP Tavern](https://wptavern.com/the-future-of-wordpress-the-block-editor-is-here-to-stay))

It was one of the most controversial changes in WordPress history.
Contemporaneous reporting noted Gutenberg's plugin received overwhelmingly
negative ratings at launch (around 2 out of 5 stars), that the release was sprung
on developers with little notice and moved earlier than expected, and that the
**Classic Editor** plugin (which restores the old editor) accumulated millions of
installs — an indicator of how many users resisted the change.
([DeliciousBrains](https://deliciousbrains.com/wordpress-gutenberg/);
[room34 blog](https://blog.room34.com/archives/8891/gutenberg-is-fundamentally-flawed-and-unsustainable/))

> **Could not verify** the exact star-rating and Classic Editor install counts to
> a primary WordPress.org snapshot at a specific date; the figures above are from
> reputable secondary coverage and should be read as approximate.

### The 2010 Thesis / GPL dispute (brief)

In **July 2010**, theme developer **Chris Pearson** (author of the premium
**Thesis** theme) and **Matt Mullenweg** clashed publicly over whether WordPress
themes must be GPL-licensed. Mullenweg's position: themes are derivative works of
WordPress and therefore must be GPL. Pearson's position: Thesis was not a
derivative work and could be sold under proprietary terms. The dispute came to a
head in a live debate on Andrew Warner's **Mixergy** podcast. Pearson ultimately
released Thesis under a **split license** — PHP code under GPL, other assets
proprietary.
([Post Status: Pearson versus Mullenweg](https://poststatus.com/pearson-versus-mullenweg-a-history/);
[WP Tavern](https://wptavern.com/mullenweg-and-pearson-square-off-on-patents-gpl-and-trademarks))

> Years later, Mullenweg reportedly purchased the **thesis.com** domain. *Could
> not independently verify the reported six-figure purchase price* beyond
> secondary "domain stories" coverage. ([Domain Stories](https://domain-stories.com/thesis_com/))

## The WP Engine war (2024–2025)

> **Framing.** What follows is a dispute in which both sides have made strong
> public accusations. Statements are attributed to the party that made them.
> The only items presented as adjudicated fact are the court's December 2024
> preliminary-injunction orders.

### Background

**WP Engine** is a large managed-WordPress hosting company. **Silver Lake**, a
private-equity firm, **invested in WP Engine in 2018.**
([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/10/05/wordpress-ceo-matt-mullenweg-goes-nuclear-on-silver-lake-wp-engine-.html))

### The opening salvo: WordCamp US 2024 and "cancer to WordPress"

At **WordCamp US 2024** in Portland (September 2024), Mullenweg publicly attacked
WP Engine. In a WordPress.org blog post titled **"WP Engine is not WordPress,"**
Mullenweg wrote that WP Engine offers customers "a cheap knock-off" and that
**"this is one of the many reasons they are a cancer to WordPress, and it's
important to remember that unchecked, cancer will spread."**
([WP Tavern](https://wptavern.com/highlights-from-matt-mullenwegs-spiciest-word-camp-presentation-at-wordcamp-us-2024);
[webhosting.today](https://webhosting.today/2024/09/23/wordcamp-us-controversy-matt-mullenweg-targets-wp-engine-calling-it-the-cancer-of-wordpress/))

**Mullenweg's allegations** (his stated grievances): WP Engine disables post
revisions by default; Silver Lake "doesn't give a dang about your Open Source
ideals"; and WP Engine under-contributes to WordPress's "Five for the Future"
program relative to its size, while profiting heavily from WordPress and causing
brand confusion.
([WP Tavern](https://wptavern.com/highlights-from-matt-mullenwegs-spiciest-word-camp-presentation-at-wordcamp-us-2024);
[CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/10/05/wordpress-ceo-matt-mullenweg-goes-nuclear-on-silver-lake-wp-engine-.html))

### The trademark / licensing-fee demand

Press reporting and the parties' filings indicate Automattic demanded WP Engine
either pay a **trademark licensing fee reported at ~8% of revenue** or make an
equivalent contribution of engineering resources. Automattic's attorneys sent WP
Engine a **cease-and-desist on/around September 23, 2024**, alleging trademark
violations.
([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/10/05/wordpress-ceo-matt-mullenweg-goes-nuclear-on-silver-lake-wp-engine-.html);
[The Register](https://www.theregister.com/2024/09/24/wp_engine_claims_automattic_ceo/))

> **Attributed, contested:** WP Engine characterized these demands as
> **extortion and abuse of power** in its complaint; Automattic characterized
> them as legitimate trademark enforcement. These are competing allegations.
> ([TechCrunch, Dec 2024](https://techcrunch.com/2024/12/10/court-orders-mullenweg-and-automattic-to-restore-wp-engines-access-to-wordpress-org/);
> [Automattic legal response](https://automattic.com/2024/10/31/legal-response-to-wp-engine/))

### Blocking WP Engine from WordPress.org

Automattic/WordPress.org **blocked WP Engine's access to WordPress.org** (its
plugin/theme update and login infrastructure). The block prevented WP Engine from
pushing updates to its plugins hosted on WordPress.org, including ACF.
([Slate](https://slate.com/technology/2024/10/wordpress-wpengine-matt-mullenweg-drama-explained.html))

### The forced takeover of ACF → "Secure Custom Fields" (Oct 2024)

On **October 12, 2024**, Mullenweg announced on WordPress.org that the WordPress
security team was **invoking "point 18 of the plugin directory guidelines" to
fork Advanced Custom Fields (ACF) into a new plugin, "Secure Custom Fields"
(SCF).** The post said SCF "has been updated to remove commercial upsells and fix
a security problem," described it as "a rare and unusual situation brought on by
WP Engine's legal attacks," and noted that sites with auto-updates enabled would
be **auto-switched** from ACF to SCF.
([WordPress.org: Secure Custom Fields](https://wordpress.org/news/2024/10/secure-custom-fields/))

> **Attributed, contested:** WP Engine and many community members described this
> as a hostile takeover of a plugin WP Engine had built and maintained; Mullenweg
> framed it as a security action permitted by directory guidelines. The same post
> also noted that Jason Bahl left WP Engine for Automattic, taking WPGraphQL to
> Automattic as a "canonical" plugin.
> ([WordPress.org](https://wordpress.org/news/2024/10/secure-custom-fields/))

### The lawsuit (Oct 2024)

**WP Engine sued Automattic and Mullenweg in early October 2024** (filed October 2,
2024), alleging (among other things) **extortion, abuse of power, and intentional
interference with contractual relations**; WP Engine also accused Mullenweg of
slander/libel over his public comments and alleged conflicts of interest in how he
controls both the community project and his for-profit company.
([Slate](https://slate.com/technology/2024/10/wordpress-wpengine-matt-mullenweg-drama-explained.html);
[NBC DFW / CNBC](https://www.nbcdfw.com/news/business/money-report/why-wordpress-founder-matt-mullenweg-has-gone-nuclear-against-tech-investing-giant-silver-lake/3663380/))

Automattic publicly called the complaint **"meritless"** and said it would file a
motion to dismiss and counterclaims.
([Automattic: Legal Response to WP Engine](https://automattic.com/2024/10/31/legal-response-to-wp-engine/))
Automattic later filed counterclaims, and WP Engine subsequently expanded its case
with antitrust claims.
([PPC Land](https://ppc.land/automattic-files-counterclaims-in-wp-engine-trademark-dispute/);
[The Repository](https://www.therepository.email/wp-engine-expands-legal-fight-against-automattic-and-matt-mullenweg-with-antitrust-claims))

### The WordPress.org login "loyalty" / affiliation checkbox

Automattic added a **checkbox to the WordPress.org login** requiring users to
**confirm they are not affiliated with WP Engine.** This was later among the
things the court ordered removed.
([TechCrunch, Dec 2024](https://techcrunch.com/2024/12/10/court-orders-mullenweg-and-automattic-to-restore-wp-engines-access-to-wordpress-org/))

### The December 2024 preliminary injunction (adjudicated)

On **December 10, 2024**, **Judge Araceli Martínez-Olguín** of the U.S. District
Court (N.D. Cal.) issued a **preliminary injunction** against Automattic and
Mullenweg, finding that **WP Engine was likely to succeed on the merits of its
intentional-interference-with-contractual-relations claim.** The court ordered
Automattic and Mullenweg to, among other things:

- **Restore WP Engine's access** to WordPress.org (themes, plugins, subdomains)
  to its state on/around September 20, before the ban;
- **Restore WP Engine's access to and control over the ACF plugin** on
  WordPress.org;
- **Remove the login checkbox** requiring users to disclaim WP Engine
  affiliation; and
- **Remove a customer-tracking page** that showed customers leaving WP Engine.

([WP Tavern](https://wptavern.com/court-grants-wp-engine-preliminary-injunction-against-automattic);
[TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2024/12/10/court-orders-mullenweg-and-automattic-to-restore-wp-engines-access-to-wordpress-org/);
[The Register](https://www.theregister.com/2024/12/11/wp_engine_wins_injunction_against/))

Automattic emphasized that the ruling was **preliminary** — "designed to maintain
the status quo," made "without the benefit of discovery, our motion to dismiss, or
the counterclaims we will be filing."
([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2024/12/10/court-orders-mullenweg-and-automattic-to-restore-wp-engines-access-to-wordpress-org/))

### Automattic buyouts: the "Alignment Offer"

In October 2024 Mullenweg offered employees who disagreed with the company's
direction a buyout. In his post **"Automattic Alignment"** he wrote that employees
who **resigned before 20:00 UTC on Thursday, October 3, 2024** would receive
**"$30,000 or six months of salary, whichever is higher."** He reported that
**"159 people took the offer, 8.4% of the company, the other 91.6% gave up $126M
of potential severance to stay!"** Those who took the offer were **not eligible
for rehire.**
([ma.tt: Automattic Alignment](https://ma.tt/2024/10/alignment/);
[TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2024/10/04/159-employees-are-leaving-automattic-as-ceos-fight-with-wp-engine-escalates/);
[The Register](https://www.theregister.com/2024/10/04/automattic_offers_dissident_employees_incentive/))

A **second buyout round** was reported later in October 2024, this time offering
nine months' severance.
([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2024/10/17/automattic-offered-employees-another-chance-to-quit-this-time-with-nine-months-severance/))

> **Both sides, fairly stated:** Mullenweg frames the WP Engine campaign as
> defending WordPress's open-source ideals against a private-equity owner that
> profits without contributing. WP Engine (and many in the community) frame it as
> one individual abusing control over shared community infrastructure
> (WordPress.org, the plugin directory, the trademark) to coerce a competitor.
> The December 2024 injunction is the only part a court has so far ruled on, and
> it went substantially in WP Engine's favor on the interference claim — but it is
> preliminary, not a final judgment.

## WooCommerce

### WooThemes (2008)

**WooThemes** was founded in **2008 in Cape Town, South Africa** by **Mark
Forrester, Magnus Jepson, and Adii (Adriaan) Pienaar.** The trio had first
"teamed up online" in **November 2007** to build a WordPress theme before
formalizing the company.
([WooCommerce: About](https://woocommerce.com/about/))

### WooCommerce launch (Sept 27, 2011) — forked from Jigoshop

**WooCommerce launched on September 27, 2011.** WooThemes hired **Mike Jolley and
James (Jay) Koster** — developers at Jigowatt, makers of the **Jigoshop** plugin —
to build a **fork of Jigoshop**, which became WooCommerce. (Per several accounts,
WooThemes forked Jigoshop after acquisition negotiations between the companies
broke down.)
([Wikipedia: WooCommerce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WooCommerce);
[WPShout: history of WooCommerce](https://wpshout.com/woocommerce-history/))

### Automattic acquisition (May 2015)

**In May 2015, Automattic acquired WooThemes and WooCommerce.** The deal was
**reported at roughly US$30 million.**
([Wikipedia: WooCommerce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WooCommerce);
[Post Status](https://poststatus.com/automattic-acquired-woocommerce-woothemes/))

> The ~$30M figure is a **reported** number from press coverage, not an officially
> disclosed price. ([Post Status](https://poststatus.com/automattic-acquired-woocommerce-woothemes/))

### Market share

By **September 2015**, WooCommerce reportedly ran on **~30% of e-commerce sites**;
W3Techs-based figures cited in 2024 put WooCommerce on roughly **9.2% of all
websites.**
([Wikipedia: WooCommerce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WooCommerce))

> Market-share figures for "share of online stores" vary widely by methodology
> and source; treat any single percentage as approximate.

## The PHP e-commerce landscape

### The substrate: PHP and LAMP

**PHP** originated with **Rasmus Lerdorf**, who announced **"Personal Home Page
Tools (PHP Tools) version 1.0" on June 8, 1995**, on the Usenet group
comp.infosystems.www.authoring.cgi. PHP grew out of CGI programs Lerdorf had
written (starting ~1993) to maintain his personal homepage and handle HTML forms
and database access (the "Personal Home Page / Forms Interpreter," PHP/FI).
([php.net: History of PHP](https://www.php.net/manual/en/history.php.php);
[Wikipedia: PHP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PHP))

PHP became the "P" in the **LAMP stack** (Linux, Apache, MySQL, PHP/Perl/Python),
the dominant open-source web substrate on which essentially all the platforms
below were built.

> **Could not verify** to a primary source the exact origin/coiner of the term
> "LAMP"; it is commonly attributed to a 1998 German computing magazine (*c't*)
> article, but this document does not cite a verified primary for that claim.

### Magento (Varien → eBay → Permira → Adobe)

- **2008:** Magento Community Edition first released by **Varien**, led by **Roy
  Rubin** (CEO) and **Yoav Kutner.**
  ([Wikipedia: Magento](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magento))
- **2010–2011:** **eBay** acquired a 49% stake (2010), then announced acquisition
  of the **remaining shares on June 6, 2011.**
  ([Wikipedia: Magento](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magento))
- **Nov 3, 2015:** After the eBay/PayPal split, Magento was spun off and sold to
  private-equity firm **Permira.**
  ([Wikipedia: Magento](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magento))
- **May 21, 2018:** **Adobe announced acquisition of Magento for $1.68 billion**;
  the deal **closed June 19, 2018.** Magento became **Adobe Commerce**.
  ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2018/05/21/adobe-to-acquire-magento-for-1-6-b/);
  [McFadyen Digital](https://mcfadyen.com/2018/07/19/adobe-officially-owns-magento-heres-what-it-means-for-site-operators/))
- **June 2020:** **Magento 1 reached end of life.**
  ([Brandastic](https://brandastic.com/blog/a-brief-history-of-magento/))

### PrestaShop (2007, French)

**PrestaShop launched in 2007** and is a French open-source PHP e-commerce
platform with a large extension/theme community.
([FireBear](https://firebearstudio.com/blog/the-best-self-hosted-e-commerce-platforms-review-magento-prestashop-zen-cart-woocommerce-opencart-cs-cart-oscommerce-spree-commerce-sellvana.html))

> **Could not verify** to a primary source the exact founders/company-of-origin of
> PrestaShop (commonly attributed to a student project at EPITECH in Paris that
> became PrestaShop SA). Treating only the **2007 / French** facts as confirmed
> here.

### OpenCart (Daniel Kerr)

**OpenCart** was revived by UK developer **Daniel Kerr** after the domain expired
in February 2005; its **first stable release (1.1.1) was published on Google Code
on February 11, 2009.** (It is a PHP e-commerce platform; some secondary sources
date its broader popularity to ~2010.)
([Wikipedia: OpenCart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenCart))

### osCommerce (2000) → Zen Cart (2003 fork)

**osCommerce** was started in **March 2000** in Germany by **Harald Ponce de
Leon**, originally as "The Exchange Project." In **2003**, part of the community
forked it; the project split into **CRE Loaded, Zen Cart, and the original
osCommerce.** **Zen Cart** is the best-known fork.
([Wikipedia: osCommerce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OsCommerce))

### Drupal Commerce (Commerce Guys / Ryan Szrama; Drupal by Dries Buytaert)

**Drupal** itself was created by **Dries Buytaert** (who remains project lead and
founded Acquia). **Drupal Commerce** was built by **Commerce Guys**, co-founded by
**Ryan Szrama** (author of the earlier **Ubercart** shopping cart) in **2009**;
**Drupal Commerce Alpha 1** was announced at **DrupalCon Copenhagen (2010).** In
**2019**, Commerce Guys rebranded to **Centarro.**
([Practical Ecommerce: Drupal Commerce founder](https://www.practicalecommerce.com/drupal-commerce-founder-on-collaborative-code-software-freedom-more);
[Commerce Guys: Alpha 1 announcement](https://commerceguys.com/about-us/news/drupal-commerce-alpha-1-announced-drupalcon-copenhagen);
[drupal.org: rszrama](https://www.drupal.org/u/rszrama))

> Note: Drupal is PHP-based; Drupal 8+ is built atop the **Symfony** framework.

### Sylius (Symfony-based)

**Sylius** is an open-source e-commerce framework built on **Symfony** components,
created by **Paweł Jędrzejewski.** Per the founder's own accounts, the project
began as a personal effort around 2010–2011 and reached its **first stable release
in 2017.** The name is a portmanteau of "Sylwia" and "Justyna."
([Symfony blog: interview with Paweł Jędrzejewski](https://symfony.com/blog/interview-with-pawe-jdrzejewski-founder-of-sylius-project);
[Sylius founder interview (BitBag)](https://bitbag.io/blog/pawel-jedrzejewski-sylius-interview))

### Shopware (German)

**Shopware** traces to **Stefan Hamann**, who founded a media company in
**Münsterland, Germany around 2000–2001** (at age 16); his brother **Sebastian
Hamann** joined, and together with **Stefan Heyne** they later founded **shopware
AG.** Shopware (PHP-based; modern versions built on Symfony) is a leading German
e-commerce platform.
([Shopware: Our Story](https://www.shopware.com/en/company/story/))

> The "2000 vs 2001" founding-year discrepancy appears between sources; Shopware's
> own "Our Story" page anchors the early history. The exact incorporation date of
> shopware AG is **not pinned to a single primary date here.**

### Spryker (German)

**Spryker** was **founded in 2014 in Berlin.** Multiple sources list the founding
team including **Boris Lokschin** and **Alexander Graf** (co-CEOs), along with
Fabian Wesner and Nils Seebach. Spryker is a PHP-based, headless/B2B-oriented
enterprise commerce platform.
([Spryker: About Us](https://spryker.com/about-us);
[Spryker: Boris Lokschin](https://spryker.com/about-us/boris-lokschin/);
[Crunchbase](https://www.crunchbase.com/organization/spryker-systems))

> The **exact founder roster** varies slightly across secondary trackers; the
> **2014 / Berlin / Lokschin & Graf** facts are well-supported. Treat the full
> co-founder list as **approximate.**

### Bagisto (Laravel)

**Bagisto** is a free, open-source **Laravel** (PHP) + **Vue.js** e-commerce
framework created by **Webkul.** Its **first release was October 31, 2018**, under
the MIT License.
([Made With Vue.js: Bagisto](https://madewithvuejs.com/bagisto);
[GitHub: bagisto/bagisto](https://github.com/bagisto/bagisto))

### Contrast: Shopify (not PHP)

The dominant *hosted* commerce competitor, **Shopify**, is **not** part of the PHP
landscape — it is built on **Ruby on Rails**. It is included here only as a
contrast: where WooCommerce, Magento, PrestaShop, etc. are self-hosted PHP
software (or PHP SaaS, in Adobe Commerce's case), Shopify is a proprietary
Rails-based SaaS.

> **Could not verify** within this research pass a single primary Shopify
> engineering source for "Rails"; this is widely and consistently reported but is
> flagged here for transparency rather than cited to a primary.
