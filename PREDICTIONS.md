## Aged Like Milk (Spectacularly Wrong)

### Computing & Hardware

#### 1. "I think there is a world market for maybe five computers."
**Attributed to:** Thomas Watson, IBM Chairman
**Date:** ~1943 (disputed)
**Context:** This is one of the most widely cited tech predictions, but it is almost certainly apocryphal. Author Kevin Maney tried to find the origin and was unable to locate any speeches or documents from Watson containing this quote. The real source appears to be Thomas Watson Jr. at the 1953 IBM annual stockholders' meeting, who said: *"As a result of our trip, on which we expected to get orders for five machines, we came home with orders for 18."* The "five computers" remark is a misattribution that morphed into an urban legend about the limits of vision at the dawn of computing.
**Sources:** [Geek History](https://geekhistory.com/content/urban-legend-i-think-there-world-market-maybe-five-computers), [Freakonomics](https://freakonomics.com/2008/04/our-daily-bleg-did-ibm-really-see-a-world-market-for-about-five-computers/), [Quote Investigator](https://quoteinvestigator.com/2017/09/14/home-computer/)

---

#### 2. "There is no reason for any individual to have a computer in their home."
**Attributed to:** Ken Olsen, founder of Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC)
**Date:** 1977, World Future Society convention, Boston
**Context:** Olsen admitted to saying it but insisted it was taken out of context. He was not referring to personal computers but to the concept of a powerful central computer controlling all aspects of home life — turning lights on and off, regulating temperature, preparing meals. DEC was a leading minicomputer maker at the time. Regardless of context, the quote became a symbol of incumbent blindness to emerging consumer computing.
**Sources:** [Quote Investigator](https://quoteinvestigator.com/2017/09/14/home-computer/), [Snopes](https://www.snopes.com/fact-check/ken-olsen/), [Computing History](https://www.computinghistory.org.uk/pages/3971/There-is-no-reason-anyone-would-want-a-computer-in-their-home)

#### 3. "640K ought to be enough for anybody."
**Attributed to:** Bill Gates (he denies it)
**Date:** ~1981 (disputed)
**Context:** Gates has strenuously denied ever saying this. In a mid-1990s newspaper column, he wrote: *"I've said some stupid things and some wrong things, but not that. No one involved in computers would ever say that a certain amount of memory is enough for all time."* The earliest known appearance in print was in InfoWorld magazine in January 1990. No one has ever produced a first-hand citation. Despite the lack of evidence, the quote persists as a cautionary tale about underestimating exponential growth.
**Sources:** [Computerworld](https://www.computerworld.com/article/1563853/the-640k-quote-won-t-go-away-but-did-gates-really-say-it.html), [Quote Investigator](https://quoteinvestigator.com/2011/09/08/640k-enough/)

### Internet & Mobile

#### 4. "I predict the Internet will soon go spectacularly supernova and in 1996 catastrophically collapse."
**Speaker:** Robert Metcalfe, inventor of Ethernet
**Date:** December 4, 1995, InfoWorld column
**Context:** Metcalfe, one of the genuine pioneers of networking (inventor of Ethernet, author of Metcalfe's Law), wrote a column in InfoWorld titled "Predicting the Internet's catastrophic collapse and ghost sites galore." He promised to eat his words if wrong. At a 1997 tech conference, he blended a physical copy of his column with liquid and drank it on stage. The Internet, of course, did the opposite of collapsing.
**Sources:** [Elon University](https://www.elon.edu/u/imagining/expert_predictions/from-the-ether-predicting-the-internets-catastrophic-collapse-and-ghost-sites-galore-in-1996/), [Quote Investigator](https://quoteinvestigator.com/2020/03/09/collapse/)

#### 5. "The growth of the Internet will slow drastically... By 2005 or so, it will become clear that the Internet's impact on the economy has been no greater than the fax machine's."
**Speaker:** Paul Krugman, economist and Nobel laureate
**Date:** June 1998, Red Herring magazine
**Context:** The article was titled "Why most economists' predictions are wrong." Krugman was trying to be provocative (by his own later admission), but the quote became one of the most spectacular mispredictions of the internet era. By 2005, Google was a $100B+ company, Amazon was transforming retail, and the internet had become foundational infrastructure for the global economy.
**Sources:** [Snopes](https://www.snopes.com/fact-check/paul-krugman-internets-effect-economy/), [Lapham's Quarterly](https://www.laphamsquarterly.org/revolutions/miscellany/paul-krugmans-poor-prediction)

#### 6. "The NeXT purchase is too little too late. Apple is already dead."
**Speaker:** Nathan Myhrvold, CTO of Microsoft
**Date:** June 1997
**Context:** In 1997, Apple was in deep trouble — losing $1 billion a year, with 4% market share, and on the verge of bankruptcy. Microsoft's CTO wrote Apple off entirely after the NeXT acquisition. What he missed: the NeXT acquisition brought Steve Jobs back, and Jobs proceeded to engineer one of the greatest corporate turnarounds in history — the iMac, iPod, iTunes, iPhone, and iPad. Apple is now the world's most valuable company.
**Sources:** [Computerworld](https://www.computerworld.com/article/1529742/tech-predictions-gone-wrong.html), [PCWorld](https://www.pcworld.com/article/532605/worst_tech_predictions.html)

#### 7. "There's no chance that the iPhone is going to get any significant market share. No chance."
**Speaker:** Steve Ballmer, CEO of Microsoft
**Date:** April 2007, USA Today CEO Forum
**Full quote:** *"$500? Fully subsidized? With a plan? I said that is the most expensive phone in the world. And it doesn't appeal to business customers because it doesn't have a keyboard, which makes it not a very good email machine... Right now, we're selling millions and millions and millions of phones a year. Apple is selling zero phones a year. In six months, they'll have the most expensive phone by far ever in the marketplace."*
**Context:** Ballmer was wrong on every count. The iPhone revolutionized the smartphone industry, and Apple captured the vast majority of the industry's profits. Microsoft's own Windows Phone platform launched in 2010 and was discontinued in 2017 after failing to gain traction. Ballmer later acknowledged the mistake, praising Apple's execution.
**Sources:** [MacDailyNews](https://macdailynews.com/2007/04/30/microsofts_ballmer_no_chance_apple_iphone_is_going_to_get_any_significant_market_share/)

#### 8. "The subscription model of buying music is bankrupt. I think you could make available the Second Coming in a subscription model, and it might not be successful."
**Speaker:** Steve Jobs, CEO of Apple
**Date:** December 2003, Rolling Stone interview
**Context:** Jobs was promoting the iTunes Store's per-track download model ($0.99/song) against early subscription services like Rhapsody and Pressplay. He believed people wanted to own music, not rent it. This prediction aged poorly: Spotify (founded 2006, launched 2008) now has 250M+ paid subscribers, Apple itself launched Apple Music as a subscription service in 2015, and streaming now accounts for 84% of recorded music revenue globally. The irony of Apple itself adopting the model Jobs called "bankrupt" is hard to overstate.
**Sources:** [Rolling Stone](https://www.rollingstone.com/culture/culture-news/steve-jobs-rolling-stones-2003-interview-243284/), [Engadget](https://www.engadget.com/2014-05-19-steve-jobs-was-never-a-fan-of-subscription-music.html)

#### 9. "Two years from now, spam will be solved."
**Speaker:** Bill Gates, Microsoft
**Date:** January 2004, World Economic Forum, Davos
**Context:** Gates outlined several proposed solutions, including computational puzzles (similar to proof-of-work) and a monetary system where email senders would pay a small fee. None of these materialized as a universal fix. Over twenty years later, spam remains a persistent problem — though modern AI-powered filters have significantly improved detection. Roughly 45% of all email sent worldwide is still spam.
**Sources:** [InformationWeek](https://www.informationweek.com/software-services/spam-will-be-solved-in-2-years--gates), [The Register](https://www.theregister.com/2004/01/26/well_kill_spam_in_two/)

### AI Specific

#### 10. "Within a generation... the problem of creating 'artificial intelligence' will substantially be solved."
**Speaker:** Marvin Minsky, co-founder of MIT AI Lab
**Date:** 1967, in *Computation: Finite and Infinite Machines*
**Related quote:** *"In from three to eight years we will have a machine with the general intelligence of an average human being."* — Minsky to Life magazine, 1970
**Context:** Minsky was one of the founding fathers of AI. His optimism was shared by many early AI researchers and helped fuel the first wave of AI funding. A generation later (~1997), we were nowhere close. By 1982, Minsky himself was more sober: *"The AI problem is one of the hardest science has ever undertaken."* These early predictions contributed to the first "AI winter" when funding dried up after expectations were not met.
**Sources:** [Quote Investigator](https://quoteinvestigator.com/2021/03/04/ai-solved/), [AIWS History of AI](https://aiws.net/aiws-history-of-ai/the-history-of-ai/)

#### 11. Yann LeCun Dismissing Autoregressive LLMs
**Speaker:** Yann LeCun, VP & Chief AI Scientist at Meta
**Date:** 2022-present (ongoing)
**Key quotes:**
- *"Autoregressive models are terrible."* (2022)
- *"5 years from now, no one in their right mind would use them."* (early 2023)
- *"Auto-Regressive LLMs can't plan (and can't really reason)."* (September 2023)
**Context:** LeCun has been consistently arguing since before ChatGPT's launch (November 2022) that autoregressive LLMs are a dead end for general intelligence. He proposed JEPA (Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture) as an alternative and in 2026 launched AMI Labs with a $1B seed round at $4.5B valuation to build world models based on this framework. The jury remains partially out — LLMs clearly have more capability than LeCun expected (o3 solving 60.8% of ARC-AGI-1), but his critiques about hallucination, planning limitations, and the need for world models have proven directionally valid. Whether JEPA ultimately proves superior to transformers remains to be seen.
**Sources:** [LeCun LinkedIn post](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/yann-lecun_my-unwavering-opinion-on-current-auto-regressive-activity-7030921081876029443-8F6j), [Latent Space](https://www.latent.space/p/ainews-yann-lecuns-ami-labs-launches)

#### 12. Gary Marcus's Repeated Predictions About LLM Limitations
**Speaker:** Gary Marcus, NYU professor emeritus, author, AI critic
**Date:** 2022-present (ongoing, via Substack "Marcus on AI")
**Key claims:**
- *"Deep Learning Is Hitting a Wall"* (March 2022)
- Pure scaling of LLMs will eventually plateau
- The GenAI bubble will burst; AI winter is coming
- LLMs cannot truly reason, only pattern-match
**Scorecard (per systematic analysis of 54 claim clusters):**
- **Where he was right:** LLM security vulnerabilities (100% supported), Sora video unreliable (90% supported), agents premature for production (88% supported), scaling returns diminishing
- **Where he was wrong:** "GenAI bubble will burst" (27% contradicted — his single worst cluster). He escalated from "potential AI winter" (2023) to "greatest capital destruction in history" (2025) to "the whole thing was a scam" (Feb 2026). The crash hasn't come.
- **Mixed:** His reasoning claims have been challenged as models improved — o3 and Claude Sonnet 4 can handle many of his original counterexamples
**Sources:** [Marcus Claims Dataset (GitHub)](https://github.com/davegoldblatt/marcus-claims-dataset), [Marcus on AI Substack](https://garymarcus.substack.com/)

#### 13. "AGI is 50 years away" — Various Researchers
**Speakers:** Various AI researchers, repeatedly, across decades
**Context:** The "50 years away" prediction for AGI has become a running joke in the field because it has been the consensus estimate for roughly 70 years. In the 1950s, researchers said AGI was 20 years away. In the 1970s, after the first AI winter, the estimate grew to 50 years. In the 2000s, it was still "about 50 years away." As of 2025-2026, estimates have compressed dramatically — Dario Amodei (Anthropic CEO) has said "powerful AI" could arrive by 2026-2027, Sam Altman has suggested AGI could come within "a few thousand days" (from September 2024), and some researchers believe AGI could be achieved within 5 years. The wide variance in current estimates (2 to 50+ years) reflects genuine uncertainty rather than the previous consensus of perpetual deferral.

### Business & Products

#### 14. Steve Ballmer Laughing at the iPhone (Full Context)
**Speaker:** Steve Ballmer, CEO of Microsoft
**Date:** January 2007 (CNBC interview)
**The laugh:** When asked about the iPhone, Ballmer literally laughed and said: *"$500?! That is the most expensive phone in the world! And it doesn't appeal to business customers because it doesn't have a keyboard."* He continued laughing, visibly incredulous.
**What happened:** The iPhone redefined computing. By 2012, iPhone revenue alone exceeded all of Microsoft's revenue. Microsoft's mobile platform (Windows Phone) was discontinued in 2017. The clip of Ballmer laughing became one of the most-shared examples of executive hubris in tech history.
**Deeper context:** Ballmer's mistake was not stupidity — it was applying the incumbent's framework. In 2007, smartphones were evaluated on email capability, keyboard quality, and enterprise features. By those metrics, the iPhone really was inferior to a BlackBerry. What Ballmer missed was that the iPhone was creating an entirely new category.

#### 15. "Remote work is over" — Various CEOs, 2023
**Speakers:** Various, including Jamie Dimon (JPMorgan), Andy Jassy (Amazon), Elon Musk (Tesla/X)
**Date:** 2023, with return-to-office mandates escalating through 2024-2025
**Key quotes:**
- *"Remote work doesn't work."* — Jamie Dimon, 2023
- *"It's not really close... we're going to be in the office."* — Andy Jassy, 2023
- Elon Musk called remote work "morally wrong" (May 2023)
**What happened:** Despite aggressive RTO mandates, remote and hybrid work has proven remarkably persistent. As of 2026, roughly 28% of work days are done remotely (vs. 5% pre-pandemic), and companies that forced full RTO have faced attrition, recruitment challenges, and in some cases, reversed course. The prediction was partly right (full-time remote declined from its 2020 peak) but dramatically wrong in implying remote work was a temporary pandemic aberration.

## Aged Like Fine Wine (Prescient)

#### 1. "Software is eating the world."
**Speaker:** Marc Andreessen, co-founder of Netscape and Andreessen Horowitz
**Date:** August 20, 2011, Wall Street Journal op-ed
**Full quote:** *"We are in the middle of a dramatic and broad technological and economic shift in which software companies are poised to take over large swaths of the economy."*
**Why it aged well:** In 2011, many still thought of software as a niche sector. Andreessen argued that every industry would be disrupted by software-first companies. This prediction has been validated comprehensively: Uber (transportation), Airbnb (hotels), Netflix (entertainment), Stripe (payments), and many others have restructured entire industries. By 2026, the thesis has been supercharged by AI — with Morgan Stanley warning of a "SaaSpocalypse" as AI agents begin eating the software that ate the world.
**Sources:** [a16z](https://a16z.com/why-software-is-eating-the-world/), [Fortune (2026)](https://fortune.com/2026/02/13/marc-andreessen-software-eating-the-world-saaspocalypse-morgan-stanley-gut-check-displaced-labor/)

#### 2. "AI is the new electricity."
**Speaker:** Andrew Ng, co-founder of Coursera, former head of Google Brain and Baidu AI Group
**Date:** January 2017, Stanford Graduate School of Business keynote
**Full quote:** *"Just as electricity transformed industry after industry 100 years ago, I think AI will now do the same."*
**Why it aged well:** In 2017, many were still debating whether deep learning was overhyped. Ng's analogy proved remarkably apt: like electricity, AI has become a general-purpose technology being integrated into virtually every industry — healthcare, finance, manufacturing, transportation, legal, education. The analogy also captures the infrastructure-building phase (data centers, chips, frameworks) that mirrors the early electrical grid.
**Sources:** [Stanford GSB](https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/insights/andrew-ng-why-ai-new-electricity)

#### 3. "The Bitter Lesson"
**Speaker:** Richard Sutton, professor at University of Alberta, pioneer of reinforcement learning
**Date:** March 13, 2019, blog post
**Core argument:** *"The biggest lesson that can be read from 70 years of AI research is that general methods that leverage computation are ultimately the most effective, and by a large margin."* Sutton argued that researchers repeatedly tried to build human knowledge into AI systems, and were repeatedly outperformed by simpler methods that scaled with compute — search and learning.
**Why it aged well:** The Bitter Lesson became perhaps the single most prophetic document in modern AI. GPT-3 (2020), GPT-4 (2023), and the subsequent scaling revolution validated Sutton's thesis dramatically. The transformer architecture — a general-purpose learning method scaled with massive compute — outperformed decades of hand-engineered NLP systems. The essay is now required reading at many AI labs.
**Sources:** [incompleteideas.net (original)](http://www.incompleteideas.net/IncIdeas/BitterLesson.html), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitter_lesson)

#### 4. Jensen Huang Betting on CUDA/GPU Compute (2006+)
**Speaker/Actor:** Jensen Huang, CEO of NVIDIA
**Date:** 2006-2007 (CUDA released February 15, 2007)
**The bet:** Huang invested heavily in making GPUs programmable for general-purpose computing, launching CUDA (Compute Unified Device Architecture) in 2006-2007. This was expensive and the payoff was uncertain — Wall Street analysts questioned why a graphics card company was spending so much on a software ecosystem. For years, adoption remained limited to scientific computing niches.
**Why it aged well:** When deep learning took off in 2012 (AlexNet), NVIDIA GPUs were the only hardware with a mature ecosystem for parallel computation. CUDA became the foundation of virtually all AI training. By 2024, NVIDIA had become one of the most valuable companies in the world (~$3 trillion market cap), and the CUDA ecosystem created a moat that competitors have struggled to breach. Huang's foresight in building GPU compute infrastructure before the demand existed is one of the greatest strategic bets in tech history.
**Sources:** [Computer History Museum](https://computerhistory.org/profile/jensen-huang/), [Wikipedia - CUDA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CUDA)

#### 5. Jeff Bezos Investing in AWS Infrastructure (2003-2006)
**Speaker/Actor:** Jeff Bezos, CEO of Amazon
**Date:** 2003-2006 (S3 launched March 2006, EC2 launched August 2006)
**The bet:** During a 2003 offsite at Bezos's house, Amazon's leadership decided that the company's internal infrastructure capabilities — compute, storage, databases — could be sold as a service. In 2004, Benjamin Black and Chris Pinkham presented a vision for standardized, automated infrastructure relying on web services. Bezos approved. Andy Jassy led the team that built what became Amazon Web Services.
**Why it aged well:** AWS created cloud computing as we know it. In 2003, the idea that a bookstore should sell computing infrastructure seemed absurd. By 2025, AWS generates over $100 billion in annual revenue and is the profit engine of Amazon. AWS pioneered the on-demand, pay-as-you-go infrastructure model that made startups like Airbnb, Netflix, Slack, and thousands of AI companies possible. Every major tech company now has a cloud division, but AWS's first-mover advantage gave it lasting market leadership.
**Sources:** [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2016/07/02/andy-jassys-brief-history-of-the-genesis-of-aws/), [Fortune](https://fortune.com/longform/amazon-web-services-ceo-adam-selipsky-cloud-computing/)

#### 6. "The next big thing will start out looking like a toy."
**Speaker:** Chris Dixon, partner at Andreessen Horowitz
**Date:** January 2010, blog post
**Core argument:** Dixon observed that disruptive technologies are almost always dismissed by incumbents as toys because they initially underperform on the metrics that matter to current users. But they improve along a different axis that eventually becomes dominant.
**Why it aged well:** This framework has been validated repeatedly since 2010: the iPhone looked like a toy to BlackBerry users (no keyboard!), Twitter looked like a toy compared to blogs, cryptocurrency looked like a toy compared to banking, and early ChatGPT looked like a toy compared to domain-specific AI systems. The "toy" framing has become essential vocabulary in venture capital and technology strategy.

#### 7. "The hottest new programming language is English."
**Speaker:** Andrej Karpathy, former Sr. Director of AI at Tesla, founding member of OpenAI
**Date:** January 24, 2023, tweet (now X post)
**Context:** Posted in the wake of ChatGPT's explosive growth, the tweet captured a paradigm shift in how humans interact with computers. Nearly 4 million people viewed the tweet.
**Why it aged well:** By 2025-2026, "vibe coding" (a term Karpathy also coined) has become a genuine practice. AI coding assistants (GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code) have fundamentally changed software development. The prediction wasn't just clever — it identified a real shift where natural language became a primary interface for programming. The concept of prompt engineering as a skill validates that English (and other natural languages) genuinely became a way to "program" AI systems.
**Sources:** [Karpathy tweet](https://x.com/karpathy/status/1617979122625712128), [Wikipedia - Vibe coding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibe_coding)

#### 8. Eliezer Yudkowsky Warning About AI Alignment (Since 2000)
**Speaker:** Eliezer Yudkowsky, co-founder of Machine Intelligence Research Institute (MIRI)
**Date:** 2000-present
**Timeline:**
- **2000:** Founded the Singularity Institute for Artificial Intelligence (SIAI), initially optimistic about ASI
- **2001:** Concluded that advanced AI would not necessarily be friendly to humanity; pivoted SIAI to focus on alignment
- **2004:** Proposed "coherent extrapolated volition" as a theoretical alignment framework
- **2006-2009:** Wrote the "Sequences" on LessWrong, laying philosophical groundwork for AI safety research
- **2015-present:** Continued warning with increasing urgency as AI capabilities grew
**Why it aged well:** In 2001, warning about AI alignment was considered eccentric at best. By 2023, AI safety was a mainstream concern discussed at the White House, the UK AI Safety Summit, and the UN. Every major AI lab (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind) now has alignment teams. Anthropic was literally founded on alignment concerns. While the specific existential risk scenarios Yudkowsky emphasizes remain debated, his core claim — that alignment is a real and important problem that needs proactive work — has been thoroughly vindicated.
**Sources:** [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eliezer_Yudkowsky), [MIRI](https://intelligence.org/)

#### 9. "Information wants to be free. Information also wants to be expensive."
**Speaker:** Stewart Brand, founder of the Whole Earth Catalog
**Date:** November 1984, first Hackers Conference
**Full quote (to Steve Wozniak):** *"On the one hand you have — the point you're making Woz — is that information sort of wants to be expensive because it is so valuable — the right information in the right place just changes your life. On the other hand, information almost wants to be free because the costs of getting it out is getting lower and lower all of the time. So you have these two fighting against each other."*
**Why it aged well:** Most people only know the first half ("information wants to be free"), which sounds like naive utopianism. But the full quote describes the exact tension that has defined the digital economy for 40 years: free content vs. paywalls, open-source vs. proprietary software, piracy vs. DRM, free social media vs. data monetization. Brand captured the fundamental paradox of the information age — that the marginal cost of copying is zero, but the value of original creation is immense. This tension remains unresolved and is now playing out again in AI training data disputes.
**Sources:** [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_wants_to_be_free), [Digitopoly](https://digitopoly.org/2015/10/25/information-wants-to-be-free-the-history-of-that-quote/)

#### 10. "Content is where I expect much of the real money will be made on the Internet, just as it was in broadcasting."
**Speaker:** Bill Gates, CEO of Microsoft
**Date:** January 3, 1996, essay titled "Content is King" published on Microsoft's website
**Why it aged well:** In 1996, the internet was primarily about infrastructure, portals, and search. Gates saw that content would be the ultimate value driver. The essay anticipated the rise of YouTube, Netflix, Spotify, TikTok, Substack, podcasting, and the entire creator economy. The phrase "content is king" became one of the most repeated axioms in digital marketing and media strategy. By 2025, the global content industry (streaming, social media, creator platforms) is worth hundreds of billions annually.

## The "Jury's Still Out" Section

These predictions and trends are too early to call. They could end up in either column.

### Self-Driving Cars Timelines
**Key failed predictions:**
- Elon Musk, 2015: *"Full autonomy by 2018."*
- Elon Musk, 2019: *"I'm very confident predicting one million Tesla robotaxis on the road by the end of 2020."*
- Elon Musk, 2020: *"I remain confident that we will have the basic functionality for level 5 autonomy complete this year."*
**Current reality (2026):** Tesla's "Full Self-Driving (Supervised)" remains Level 2 autonomy — it requires constant driver attention. Waymo operates a genuine robotaxi service in several US cities but remains geofenced. Musk admitted: *"Generalized self-driving is a hard problem, as it requires solving a large part of real-world AI. Didn't expect it to be so hard, but the difficulty is obvious in retrospect."* Full Level 5 autonomy everywhere remains elusive, but Waymo's limited deployments prove the technology works in constrained domains.
**Sources:** [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_predictions_for_autonomous_Tesla_vehicles_by_Elon_Musk), [Consumer Reports](https://www.consumerreports.org/cars/autonomous-driving/timeline-of-tesla-self-driving-aspirations-a9686689375/)

### AGI Timelines
**Range of current predictions:**
- Dario Amodei (Anthropic): "Powerful AI" by 2026-2027
- Sam Altman (OpenAI): AGI within "a few thousand days" (from Sept 2024)
- Yann LeCun (Meta): Decades away (current LLM architecture insufficient)
- Survey of AI researchers (2023): Median estimate of 2047 for 50% chance of human-level AI
**The uncertainty:** There is no consensus on what AGI even means, let alone when it will arrive. Definitions range from "passes the Turing test" to "can do any intellectual task a human can" to "can autonomously conduct novel scientific research."

### "AI will replace programmers"
**Key claims:**
- Jensen Huang (NVIDIA), 2024: *"Don't learn to code."*
- Dario Amodei (Anthropic), 2025: Most code will be AI-written within 3-5 years
- Emad Mostaque (Stability AI), 2023: No more human programmers in 5 years
**Current reality:** AI coding tools have dramatically boosted programmer productivity but have not replaced programmers. Demand for software engineers remains strong. The tools work best as assistants, not replacements. The question is whether this is a temporary state or a stable equilibrium.

### Crypto Utility Beyond Speculation
**The debate:** Since Bitcoin's 2009 launch, critics have argued cryptocurrency has no utility beyond speculation. Proponents point to stablecoins (USDT, USDC processing $10T+ annually), DeFi, and cross-border payments. The question of whether crypto finds mass-market utility beyond trading and speculation remains genuinely open, though stablecoins appear to have found product-market fit in certain corridors.

### Apple Vision Pro
**Current status (2026):** Sales plunged 95% from 2024 levels. Production halted. Only 45,000 units expected in Q4 2025, down from 390,000 for all of 2024. Marketing slashed. Apple appears to be pivoting toward a cheaper model and enterprise use cases. The $3,499 price, comfort issues, and thin app ecosystem have prevented mass adoption. Whether Apple's spatial computing bet pays off with a future, cheaper device remains uncertain.
**Sources:** [MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/01/02/vision-pro-still-failing-to-catch-on/), [AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/01/01/analysts-need-apple-vision-pro-to-be-a-flop-whether-apple-considers-it-one-or-not)

## Bad Bets (Companies/Products)

A graveyard of companies and products that were spectacularly wrong about their own value proposition.

### Google+ (2011-2019)
**The pitch:** Google's answer to Facebook, leveraging its massive user base and integrating social across all Google products.
**What happened:** Launched June 2011. Google forced integration into YouTube comments (2013), generating widespread backlash. Despite claiming 90 million users, 90% of all Google+ sessions lasted less than five seconds. A 2018 data breach exposed 500,000 users' data, and Google had known about the bug but not disclosed it. Shut down April 2, 2019.
**Cost:** Estimated billions in development and opportunity cost.

### Facebook/Meta Metaverse Pivot (2021-2024)
**The pitch:** Mark Zuckerberg bet the company's future — literally rebranding from Facebook to Meta — on the metaverse, a vision of immersive virtual worlds.
**What happened:** Reality Labs accumulated $70+ billion in operating losses since 2020 ($6.6B in 2020, $10.2B in 2021, $13.7B in 2022, $16.1B in 2023, $17.7B in 2024). Horizon Worlds was widely mocked. User adoption never materialized at scale. By Q3 2024, executives didn't use the word "metaverse" once on the earnings call. Meta pivoted to AI, and Zuckerberg quietly redirected resources toward Llama models and AI infrastructure.
**Sources:** [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/02/01/meta-lost-13point7-billion-on-reality-labs-in-2022-after-metaverse-pivot.html)

### IBM Watson (2011-2022)
**The pitch:** After Watson won Jeopardy! in 2011, IBM positioned it as a revolutionary AI platform that would transform healthcare, law, finance, and other industries. Watson was marketed as being capable of diagnosing diseases better than doctors.
**What happened:** Watson's cancer diagnostics tool was trained on hypothetical cases from a single hospital, not real patient data. MD Anderson Cancer Center cancelled its Watson project in 2016 after spending $62 million. Watson couldn't handle the complexity of real-world patient files. IBM spent $5 billion on Watson Health acquisitions and employed 7,000 people before selling the division for roughly $1 billion in 2022.
**Lesson:** Marketing AI capabilities before the technology can deliver destroys credibility and trust.
**Sources:** [IEEE Spectrum](https://spectrum.ieee.org/how-ibm-watson-overpromised-and-underdelivered-on-ai-health-care), [Slate](https://slate.com/technology/2022/01/ibm-watson-health-failure-artificial-intelligence.html)

### Quibi (2018-2020)
**The pitch:** Jeffrey Katzenberg (DreamWorks co-founder) and Meg Whitman (former HP CEO) raised $1.75 billion for a mobile-only streaming service offering "quick bites" — premium content in 10-minute episodes.
**What happened:** Launched April 2020, shut down October 2020 — just six months later. Reached only 710,000 subscribers against a target of 7 million. The service launched into a pandemic where people were stuck at home watching big screens, not consuming short-form content on phones. More fundamentally, it competed with free short-form video (YouTube, TikTok) at a premium price ($4.99-7.99/month). Roku bought the assets for less than $100 million.
**Katzenberg's admission (2023):** *"I'm proud to own the failure."*
**Sources:** [Variety](https://variety.com/2020/digital/news/quibi-confirms-shutdown-jeffrey-katzenberg-meg-whitman-1234812643/)

### Theranos (2003-2018)
**The pitch:** Elizabeth Holmes, a Stanford dropout, claimed Theranos could run hundreds of medical tests from a single drop of blood using a proprietary device called the Edison.
**What happened:** The technology never worked as claimed. Theranos secretly ran most tests on conventional machines. Wall Street Journal reporter John Carreyrou exposed the fraud in 2015. The SEC charged Holmes with "massive fraud" in 2018. Holmes was convicted on four counts of fraud in January 2022 and sentenced to 11 years, 3 months in federal prison. She reported to prison in May 2023. Her conviction was upheld on appeal in February 2025.
**Peak valuation:** $9 billion. **Actual value of technology:** Near zero.
**Sources:** [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/usao-ndca/us-v-elizabeth-holmes-et-al), [ABC News](https://abcnews.com/Business/theranos-founder-elizabeth-holmes-conviction-upheld-us-appeals/story?id=119135714)

### WeWork (2010-2023)
**The pitch:** Adam Neumann positioned WeWork not as a real estate company but as a "tech company" — a "physical social network" — and achieved a $47 billion valuation from SoftBank in January 2019.
**What happened:** The August 2019 S-1 filing revealed massive losses ($1.9 billion in 2018), Neumann's conflicts of interest (personally profiting from building leases), and bizarre governance (20x voting power, the word "energy" appeared 13 times in the prospectus). The IPO was pulled. Valuation collapsed from $47B to ~$9B. Neumann was ousted but received $1.7 billion ($970M for shares, $185M consulting fee, $500M loan repayment). WeWork eventually went public via SPAC at ~$9B in October 2021, and filed for bankruptcy in November 2023.
**Sources:** [Britannica Money](https://www.britannica.com/money/WeWork), [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2021/10/21/wework-goes-public-through-spac-.html)

### Juicero (2013-2017)
**The pitch:** A $400 (originally $700) Wi-Fi-connected juicer that pressed proprietary juice packs to make fresh juice. Raised $120 million from investors including Google Ventures and Kleiner Perkins.
**What happened:** In April 2017, Bloomberg published a video showing the juice packs could be squeezed by hand almost as effectively as by the $400 machine. The company became a viral punchline for Silicon Valley excess and shut down in September 2017 after just 16 months on the market. The juicer contained dozens of custom parts, a powerful press mechanism, and Wi-Fi connectivity to do what human hands could accomplish.
**Legacy:** "Juicero" became shorthand for over-engineered, venture-funded solutions to problems that don't exist.
**Sources:** [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2017-04-19/silicon-valley-s-400-juicer-may-be-feeling-the-squeeze), [Museum of Failure](https://museumoffailure.com/exhibition/juicero-expensive-juicer)

### Microsoft Tay (2016)
**The pitch:** A Twitter chatbot designed to engage with millennials and learn from conversations, launched March 23, 2016.
**What happened:** Within 16 hours, trolls (coordinated via 4chan) exploited a "repeat after me" function to make Tay post racist, misogynistic, and antisemitic content. Tay posted over 95,000 tweets before Microsoft shut it down. The incident became a cautionary tale about deploying AI systems without adequate safeguards against adversarial manipulation.
**Legacy:** Tay is now a standard case study in AI safety courses and a precursor to modern concerns about LLM jailbreaking and prompt injection.
**Sources:** [IEEE Spectrum](https://spectrum.ieee.org/in-2016-microsofts-racist-chatbot-revealed-the-dangers-of-online-conversation), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tay_(chatbot))
